# Desire Terms

These terms extend the [KEY Lexicon](KEY-LEXICON.md) and define the motivational structures introduced in [Chapter 19 — Desire](../manifesto/19-DESIRE.md).

---

## Desire

An enduring motivational force that draws a person or collective toward a preferred future, value, relationship, identity, condition, or experience.

Desire generates goals, directs attention, shapes acceptable risk, and influences which possibilities an agent attempts to make real. It is not identical to any single objective or action.

---

## Core Desire

A comparatively deep and persistent desire that continues generating goals across changing circumstances.

A Core Desire may concern belonging, freedom, recognition, safety, purpose, love, control, justice, continuity, or another fundamental preferred condition. It may be understood clearly, misinterpreted, denied, or remain partially hidden from the person who possesses it.

---

## Surface Goal

A concrete objective pursued under current conditions in service of one or more deeper desires.

A Surface Goal may succeed, fail, become impossible, or be replaced while the underlying desire remains active. Characters should not be treated as psychologically resolved merely because a Surface Goal has been completed.

---

## Desire Level

The depth at which a desire operates within a person's motivational structure.

KEY distinguishes among Immediate Desire, Relational Desire, Identity Desire, and Existential Desire. These levels may align, reinforce one another, or enter conflict.

---

## Immediate Desire

A desire focused on the present moment or near-term condition, such as survival, relief, escape, victory, food, rest, or possession.

Immediate Desire can dominate behavior under pressure without necessarily representing the person's deepest values or long-term identity.

---

## Relational Desire

A desire concerning another person, group, or community, including belonging, protection, forgiveness, intimacy, recognition, approval, reconciliation, or shared future.

Relational Desire creates vulnerability because its satisfaction depends partly upon agents who possess their own agency.

---

## Identity Desire

A desire concerning who a person believes they are, wishes to become, refuses to become, or hopes others will understand them to be.

Identity Desire may produce goals involving honor, worth, legacy, independence, moral consistency, status, transformation, or escape from an inherited identity.

---

## Existential Desire

A desire concerning meaning, mortality, freedom, purpose, peace, transcendence, continuity, or the significance of one's existence.

Existential Desire may remain unnamed while shaping long spans of behavior and interpretation.

---

## Competing Desires

Two or more desires within the same agent that cannot all be fully satisfied under current conditions.

Competing Desires generate internal conflict and require situated prioritization rather than arbitrary inconsistency.

---

## Desire Tension

The changing motivational pressure produced by the relative strength, compatibility, urgency, and perceived attainability of multiple desires.

Desire Tension helps determine which desire becomes dominant at a particular moment and why a person's behavior may change without violating continuity.

---

## Dominant Desire

The desire exerting the greatest influence upon an agent's current attention, risk tolerance, priorities, and choice.

A Dominant Desire is contextual rather than permanently fixed. Pressure, knowledge, memory, relationship, fear, success, failure, and revelation may change which desire dominates.

---

## Hidden Desire

A desire that is unconscious, denied, misidentified, concealed, or not yet fully understood by the person who possesses it.

A Hidden Desire must leave prior causal and emotional evidence through patterns of choice, reaction, sacrifice, avoidance, attachment, or replacement goals. It may not be invented retroactively merely to justify behavior.

---

## Desire Hierarchy

The current ordering of an agent's desires by depth, urgency, emotional force, commitment, and willingness to sacrifice.

A Desire Hierarchy is dynamic. It may change through experience while remaining continuous with the person's history and present Epistemic State.

---

## Desire Satisfaction

The degree to which an achieved condition fulfills the deeper desire that motivated its pursuit.

Completing a Surface Goal does not guarantee Desire Satisfaction. A crown may satisfy the goal of accession while failing to satisfy the desire for respect, safety, love, or significance.

---

## Desire Transformation

An earned change in what a person wants, why they want it, how they interpret it, or the means they consider acceptable in pursuing it.

Desire Transformation may follow success, failure, revelation, love, betrayal, loss, aging, reflection, or sustained pressure. It must emerge through causal and emotional history rather than arbitrary character revision.

---

## Motivational Causality

The traceable relationship between desire, perceived possibility, choice, action, and resulting change in the world.

Motivational Causality allows KEY to represent desire not as decorative character metadata, but as a causal force participating in the evolution of Canonical World State and the Possibility Graph.

---

## Governing Principle

> **People do not act because the story requires movement. They act because some possible future has become more meaningful than remaining unchanged.**
