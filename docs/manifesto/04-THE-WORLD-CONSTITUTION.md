# The World Constitution

## Book II — The World

A KEY world cannot be a collection of attractive ideas loosely held together by generated prose.

It must possess a constitution.

Not a political document, although politics may be part of it. A world constitution is the set of truths, laws, limits, causes, and relationships that determine what the world is capable of becoming.

It is the foundation beneath every scene.

Before KEY asks what happens next, it must know what is true now.

> **A world may change dramatically, but it must never become arbitrary.**

## The constitution exists before the story

The world is not created one scene at a time.

Its deepest truths must exist before the player encounters them:

- the nature of reality
- the origin and limits of magic
- the forces that shaped geography
- the histories that produced current nations
- the institutions that hold power
- the resources that create dependence
- the beliefs people inherit
- the contradictions societies refuse to confront
- the hidden truths behind public explanations
- the pressures already moving toward crisis

The player may never discover every element of this foundation.

That does not make the undiscovered parts unnecessary.

A world feels larger than the player when it contains truths that do not exist merely to be revealed.

## The constitution is not a script

A constitution defines possibility without dictating outcome.

It does not state:

> The northern kingdom will invade in the third year.

It states:

- the northern kingdom is losing arable land
- its ruling dynasty depends on military legitimacy
- its southern neighbor controls the only reliable river passage
- its people believe the valley was stolen from their ancestors
- its current monarch fears appearing weaker than the previous king

From those truths, invasion becomes possible.

So do negotiation, internal revolt, migration, religious reform, assassination, alliance, or collapse.

The constitution creates pressure. Characters and players determine how that pressure resolves.

## Hard truths and soft truths

KEY should distinguish between different kinds of world truth.

### Hard truths

These are foundational facts that cannot change without transforming the nature of the world itself.

Examples include:

- whether gods objectively exist
- whether the dead can return
- whether time can be altered
- what magic fundamentally draws upon
- whether prophecy reveals, influences, or invents the future
- whether consciousness can survive outside the body

Hard truths protect coherence.

They may be hidden, misunderstood, or disputed, but the engine must know what they are.

### Soft truths

These are enduring conditions that can change through history.

Examples include:

- which dynasty rules
- which religion dominates
- where borders lie
- who controls an artifact
- whether two peoples remain enemies
- which language is considered sacred
- whether magic is legal

Soft truths give the player and the world room to create history.

The distinction matters because KEY must know which changes represent ordinary consequence and which changes require a true alteration of reality.

## Rules require reasons

A world rule should never exist only because a designer thought it sounded interesting.

Every major rule should answer four questions:

1. **What is the rule?**
2. **Why is it true?**
3. **What does it make possible?**
4. **What does it make impossible or costly?**

Suppose resurrection exists.

That fact alone is not enough.

KEY must know:

- who can perform it
- what it costs
- whether the returned person is unchanged
- why death still matters
- how religion responds
- how inheritance law responds
- whether rulers preserve bodies
- whether murder becomes easier or more horrifying
- whether the dead remember what they experienced

A rule becomes part of the world only when its consequences spread beyond the mechanic that introduced it.

## Limits create identity

A world is defined as much by what cannot happen as by what can.

Unlimited possibility creates weightlessness.

If magic can solve any problem, then politics, sacrifice, distance, illness, secrecy, and death become difficult to believe.

If technology advances whenever the story needs a new device, then invention loses history.

If characters can know anything at any time, mystery disappears.

Limits create strategy, culture, fear, longing, and ingenuity.

The constitution should therefore define:

- physical limits
- magical limits
- informational limits
- social limits
- moral limits
- economic limits
- geographic limits
- temporal limits

These limits should not merely restrict the player. They should shape civilization.

A mountain range changes trade and language.

A magical cost changes who is willing to use power.

A taboo changes what people conceal.

A limited resource changes diplomacy.

A dangerous journey changes the meaning of distance.

## Exceptions must deepen the rule

Exceptions are powerful because they reveal what a world considers impossible.

But an exception should never exist merely to rescue a scene.

When something breaks an established rule, KEY must ask:

- Was the rule misunderstood?
- Is this a rare but legitimate exception?
- Has someone altered reality?
- Is the event deceptive?
- What cost made the exception possible?
- Who will react when the exception becomes known?

A person returning from true death should not merely surprise the player.

It should destabilize theology, law, identity, inheritance, and fear.

The exception earns its place by creating more coherence, not less.

## The constitution must generate consequences across domains

KEY should resist isolated systems.

A meaningful world event rarely affects only one category.

A magical drought may alter:

- agriculture
- warfare
- migration
- religion
- trade
- class power
- public trust
- criminal activity
- the legitimacy of rulers

A newly discovered continent may alter:

- maps
- wealth
- disease
- mythology
- naval power
- slavery
- diplomacy
- scholarship
- the meaning of ancient prophecies

The constitution should encode relationships between domains so that change can travel through the world.

This is how consequences become history rather than isolated stat adjustments.

## The world must contain competing interpretations

A constitution defines objective truth, but people do not experience objective truth directly.

They experience:

- tradition
- memory
- propaganda
- faith
- scholarship
- rumor
- trauma
- self-interest
- incomplete evidence

Different cultures may interpret the same hard truth in incompatible ways.

A celestial event may be understood as:

- divine judgment
- a natural cycle
- the return of an imprisoned being
- proof of royal legitimacy
- the beginning of the end

The engine must protect the difference between what is true and what people believe.

This allows disagreement without requiring one side to be foolish.

It also allows the player to discover that a false belief may still hold a society together, while a true revelation may destroy it.

## Stability and pressure

Every generated world should begin with both structure and instability.

Structure explains why the world has not already collapsed.

Instability explains why a story can begin now.

The constitution should identify stabilizing forces such as:

- law
- tradition
- trade dependency
- military deterrence
- shared religion
- marriage alliances
- fear of a greater enemy
- control of information
- charismatic leadership

It should also identify active pressures such as:

- succession uncertainty
- resource scarcity
- forbidden knowledge
- technological or magical disruption
- unresolved war
- generational resentment
- environmental change
- emerging faiths
- hidden corruption
- the weakening of an ancient protection

A believable world is neither static nor randomly chaotic.

It is held together by forces that are beginning to fail.

## The constitution must be testable

A world constitution is not complete because it sounds evocative.

It must survive questions.

For every major world, KEY should be able to answer:

- Why does this society exist here?
- Why has this conflict not already been resolved?
- Who benefits from the current order?
- Who pays its hidden cost?
- What does this culture fear losing?
- What can power not accomplish?
- What truth would destabilize the world if revealed?
- Which current pressure is most likely to create irreversible change?
- How would the world continue if the player did nothing?

If the engine cannot answer those questions, the world is not yet ready to carry a story.

## Constitutional change

Some player choices should be capable of changing more than politics or relationships.

The rarest events may alter the constitution itself.

Examples include:

- ending the source of magic
- killing or creating a god
- breaking the boundary between life and death
- changing the nature of prophecy
- merging two worlds
- freeing a force that rewrites physical law

These events must be treated differently from ordinary state changes.

They should require extraordinary causes, extraordinary costs, and extraordinary consequences.

A constitutional change creates a new version of the world.

Afterward, old assumptions fail. Institutions adapt or collapse. Characters reinterpret their lives. New possibilities appear, and familiar possibilities disappear.

The world does not merely continue after such an event.

It becomes something it has never been before.

## The governing principle

KEY must always be capable of distinguishing between:

- what is true
- what is believed
- what is possible
- what is forbidden
- what is changing
- what would be required to change it

That distinction is the beginning of world intelligence.

> **The world constitution is the promise that nothing important happens without a cause, nothing impossible becomes possible without a cost, and no true change remains isolated from the world it transforms.**

This is the foundation upon which living history can begin.
