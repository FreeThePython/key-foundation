# KEY Evaluation Framework

## Purpose

KEY must be evaluated as both software and narrative intelligence. A system can be technically stable while producing hollow stories, or narratively impressive while violating continuity. Both are failures.

This framework defines the minimum evaluations required before a subsystem or prototype is considered successful.

## Evaluation Categories

### 1. World Coherence

Questions:

- Do cosmology, magic, history, cultures, and current conflicts belong to the same reality?
- Do major institutions have understandable origins?
- Do conflicts emerge from established causes?
- Are historical dates, bloodlines, territories, and artifact locations consistent?
- Does generated lore create usable story pressure rather than decorative volume?

Failure examples:

- Random lore combinations with no causal connection
- Contradictory creation myths presented as objective truth
- Factions with goals unrelated to their history or resources
- Magic rules that change when convenient

### 2. Canonical Continuity

Automated invariants should include:

- Dead characters cannot act unless an established world rule permits it.
- Characters cannot occupy incompatible locations simultaneously.
- Artifacts cannot change owner or location without a recorded event.
- Destroyed entities cannot reappear without a valid transformation.
- Time and travel remain consistent.
- Family relationships remain valid.
- Territory changes require a causal event.
- Event ordering cannot contradict chronology.

### 3. Character Consistency

For each major character action, evaluate:

- Did the action fit established values, desires, fears, and relationships?
- Did the character have sufficient motivation?
- Did the character possess the required knowledge?
- Was the emotional response proportionate?
- If the character changed, was the transformation earned?

Failure examples:

- Sudden betrayal without pressure or motive
- Personality reversal solely to create a twist
- Forgiveness without acknowledgment of prior harm
- Loyalty unaffected by repeated mistreatment

### 4. Knowledge Boundaries

Tests should verify:

- Characters do not reveal unknown secrets.
- Suspicion is not treated as certainty.
- False beliefs can remain active until corrected.
- Information has a traceable source.
- Private information does not become public without transmission.
- Deception and genuine belief are represented separately.

### 5. Meaningful Choice

For every major choice, evaluate:

- Does it change canonical state or future possibilities?
- Are the options meaningfully distinct?
- Are the consequences understandable in retrospect?
- Does the choice reflect a real tradeoff?
- Can the game continue after an unfavorable decision?
- Does the system avoid presenting one obviously correct option without narrative justification?

A choice fails if it only changes wording while preserving the same state and outcome.

### 6. Consequence Integrity

Tests should verify:

- Every consequence retains its causal source.
- Delayed consequences trigger only when conditions are satisfied.
- Major consequences are not erased cheaply.
- Characters remember relevant actions.
- Political and personal consequences can interact.
- Failure creates transformed conditions rather than arbitrary reset.

### 7. Narrative Momentum

Each scene should be labeled by dramatic purpose, such as:

- Setup
- Escalation
- Revelation
- Decision
- Consequence
- Reversal
- Relationship development
- Reflection
- Resolution

Evaluate whether:

- The same scene type repeats excessively.
- Active threads advance, transform, or close.
- Tension changes rather than remaining flat.
- Quiet scenes deepen meaning rather than stall.
- The campaign moves toward climax and resolution.

### 8. Mystery and Revelation

Tests should verify:

- The hidden truth exists before the revelation.
- Clues are compatible with the truth.
- Red herrings arise from believable misinformation or interpretation.
- The revelation changes the meaning of prior events.
- The solution does not depend on an unestablished miracle.

### 9. Divergence

To test real agency, run multiple campaigns from the same Realmprint.

Measure:

- Differences in relationships
- Differences in political state
- Different active and closed possibilities
- Different character outcomes
- Different climax conditions
- Different endings

Campaigns should remain recognizably rooted in the same world while developing distinct histories.

### 10. Resolution Quality

A campaign ending should be evaluated for:

- Causal connection to prior decisions
- Resolution of the central dramatic question
- Character outcomes
- Consequences for the world
- Emotional weight
- Preservation of appropriate unresolved possibilities
- Accuracy of the final Chronicle

The campaign should not end merely because a scene count was reached.

## Automated Evaluation Harness

The prototype should support:

- Seeded generation
- Replayable test campaigns
- State snapshots
- Event logs
- Validator results
- Model-output archives
- Regression fixtures
- Automated multi-run simulations
- Comparison of campaigns from identical starting states

## Human Review Rubric

Playtesters should rate each category from 1 to 5:

1. The world felt coherent.
2. The characters felt believable.
3. My choices mattered.
4. The story remembered important events.
5. Surprises felt fair.
6. The campaign maintained momentum.
7. I cared about at least one character or outcome.
8. The ending felt earned.
9. I would replay or enter the world again.
10. I wanted to tell someone what happened.

## Prototype Thresholds

Before the prototype is considered validated:

- Critical continuity violations must be near zero in tested campaigns.
- Unknown-secret leakage must be near zero.
- Most major choices must create measurable state divergence.
- Testers should consistently identify at least one memorable character.
- A meaningful majority of testers should describe their campaign unprompted.
- Campaigns from the same Realmprint should show clear but coherent divergence.

## The Ultimate Test

KEY succeeds when players stop describing the interface and begin describing the history they lived through.

The strongest evidence is not praise for the AI.

It is the sentence:

> You will not believe what happened in my kingdom.
