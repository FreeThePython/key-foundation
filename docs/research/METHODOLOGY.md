# KEY Narrative Research Methodology

## Purpose

This document defines how the Narrative Observatory turns stories, audience response, and interactive experience into evidence that can improve KEY.

The methodology is designed to avoid three failures:

1. treating taste as fact
2. treating popularity as quality
3. treating elegant theory as proof

The goal is not to produce a universal score. The goal is to produce structured, comparable, falsifiable observations.

## Unit of Study

A study may examine:

- a complete work
- an episode, chapter, act, quest, or campaign
- a character arc
- a relationship
- a revelation sequence
- an ending
- a script and its produced adaptation
- multiple playthroughs of an interactive work
- a comparative cohort

Every study must state its unit of analysis before interpretation begins.

## Evidence Layers

### 1. Work Evidence

Directly observable material within the work:

- events
- dialogue and stated belief
- character decisions
- causal dependencies
- information available to each character
- world-state changes
- relationship-state changes
- setup and payoff
- persistent and abandoned consequences

### 2. Craft Interpretation

Analytical claims about how the work functions:

- coherence
- agency
- pressure
- momentum
- preparation
- transformation
- resolution
- thematic development

Interpretation must be linked to work evidence and labeled as interpretation.

### 3. Audience Response

Evidence may include:

- surveys
- reviews
- discussion archives
- rewatch or replay behavior
- reported emotional response
- retention and completion data
- disagreement among audience segments

Audience response describes experience. It does not automatically establish craft quality.

### 4. Critical and Scholarly Response

Evidence may include professional criticism, scholarship, awards discourse, craft analysis, and historical interpretation.

Expert reception is neither infallible nor interchangeable with audience response.

### 5. Market and Exposure Context

Record factors such as:

- release scale
- marketing
- distribution
- franchise recognition
- competition
- pricing
- platform availability
- production constraints
- adaptation expectations

Commercial outcomes must not be attributed to narrative quality without evidence.

### 6. Historical Response

Record whether reception changed over time through:

- reevaluation
- cult status
- changing cultural norms
- influence on later works
- durability
- loss or growth of reputation

## Analytical Frames

### World Engine

Examine:

- objective truth
- active history
- causal consistency
- institutions and incentives
- culture and belief
- constraints
- possibility
- continuity
- independent world activity

### Human Engine

For each major agent, examine:

- situated knowledge
- memory
- belief
- desire
- fear
- identity
- relationships
- agency
- choice
- growth
- legacy

### Story Engine

Examine:

- narrative pressure
- Narrative Anchors
- perceived possibilities
- consequential choice
- momentum
- revelation
- reinterpretation
- failure
- transformation
- convergence
- resolution
- consequence memory

## Observation Protocol

### Step 1 — Define the Question

State the research question before analysis.

Bad:

> Why is this story great?

Better:

> How does the work prepare its central revelation, and how does that preparation affect reinterpretation of earlier events?

### Step 2 — Record Context

Document medium, genre, audience, release era, production context, adaptation status, and known limitations.

### Step 3 — Build a Causal Record

Track significant events as:

```text
Prior State → Pressure → Choice or Event → Immediate Consequence → Persistent Consequence
```

Distinguish coincidence, imposed event, autonomous choice, and systemic consequence.

### Step 4 — Build Character State Records

At meaningful transitions, record:

```text
Knowledge
Belief
Desire
Fear
Identity
Relationship State
Perceived Possibilities
Chosen Action
Resulting Change
```

### Step 5 — Track Information

For important truths, record:

- what is objectively true
- who knows it
- who believes a false version
- how information travels
- when visible effects appear
- when discovery occurs
- what is reinterpreted afterward

### Step 6 — Track Pressure and Choice

Identify:

- source of pressure
- available alternatives
- whether alternatives are genuinely viable
- what the character believes is possible
- the cost of each path
- whether the final choice follows from character state

### Step 7 — Track Consequence Memory

Test whether meaningful actions continue to affect:

- world state
- relationships
- identity
- knowledge
- future opportunity
- institutional behavior
- emotional state

### Step 8 — Analyze Resolution

Ask:

- which pressures were resolved
- which remained open
- what irreversible change occurred
- whether the ending follows from accumulated causality
- whether emotional and world consequences persist
- whether closure depends on unexplained intervention

### Step 9 — Add Reception Evidence

Only after the internal analysis is recorded should reception data be used to compare predictions with lived response.

This reduces hindsight contamination.

### Step 10 — Register Findings

Each conclusion must be classified as:

- observation
- interpretation
- hypothesis
- counterexample
- unresolved question
- proposed system implication

## Comparative Design

Comparisons should isolate meaningful variation where possible.

Useful comparisons include:

- same genre, different reception
- similar premise, different character causality
- original and adaptation
- script and produced work
- early and late seasons
- linear and interactive versions
- different cultures addressing similar pressures
- multiple playthroughs of the same system

A comparison must record major confounds rather than pretending they do not exist.

## Scoring Policy

Scores are optional and subordinate to evidence.

A score may be used only when:

- the construct is defined
- evidence is cited
- confidence is recorded
- disagreement is preserved
- the score supports comparison rather than replacing explanation

No overall narrative-quality score should be treated as canonical.

## Confidence Scale

### Low

Limited evidence, substantial ambiguity, or interpretation strongly dependent on context.

### Moderate

Multiple supporting observations with plausible alternatives remaining.

### High

Repeated, direct, and converging evidence with few credible alternative explanations.

Confidence is not importance. A highly important claim may still have low confidence.

## Hypothesis Standards

A valid hypothesis must state:

1. the proposed relationship
2. the units or contexts being compared
3. the observable evidence expected
4. evidence that would weaken or falsify it
5. known confounds
6. current status

Statuses:

- Proposed
- Under Study
- Provisionally Supported
- Mixed
- Provisionally Rejected
- Revised
- Retired

No hypothesis becomes a KEY principle through repetition alone.

## Inter-Rater Disagreement

Researchers should not erase disagreement by averaging incompatible interpretations.

Record:

- the competing readings
- the evidence supporting each
- whether the disagreement concerns fact, definition, causality, or value
- whether additional evidence could resolve it

Persistent disagreement may reveal a missing concept in the framework.

## Interactive Narrative Requirements

Interactive works require additional evidence:

- number and diversity of playthroughs
- player knowledge versus character knowledge
- actual versus perceived agency
- path convergence
- state persistence
- system intervention
- off-screen agent behavior
- replay variation
- whether consequences are cosmetic, local, or systemic

A single playthrough cannot establish the behavior of an interactive system.

## Bias Controls

Every study must consider:

- survivorship bias
- popularity bias
- prestige bias
- franchise bias
- recency bias
- cultural and linguistic narrowness
- genre prejudice
- adaptation bias
- model-as-judge bias
- retrospective inevitability

## Copyright and Research Boundaries

The Observatory studies principles, structures, causal relations, and audience response. It should not reproduce substantial copyrighted text or train the framework to imitate protected expression.

Summaries should be analytical and limited to what is necessary for evidence.

## Translation Into KEY

A finding may affect KEY only after it is expressed as one or more of:

- a clarified concept
- a schema requirement
- a system invariant
- a design constraint
- an evaluation criterion
- a benchmark scenario
- a falsifiable implementation hypothesis

Research findings do not modify the Constitution automatically.

## Core Rule

> **Observe before judging. Separate evidence from interpretation. Preserve disagreement. Let the framework change when the evidence demands it.**
