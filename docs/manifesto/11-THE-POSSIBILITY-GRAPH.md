# The Possibility Graph

## Story does not move through branches

Most interactive stories are built as paths.

A choice opens one branch. Another choice closes it. The player advances through a structure whose major possibilities were decided before play began.

KEY begins from a different premise:

> **The world does not contain a hidden script. It contains conditions from which many futures may become possible.**

A king dies.

That event does not activate a single succession storyline.

It changes the world.

The queen may claim the throne. An heir may return from exile. The army may declare emergency rule. The church may challenge the bloodline. A neighboring kingdom may invade. A hidden child may become politically valuable. The capital may riot. A provincial governor may refuse taxation. A trusted advisor may destroy evidence that could settle the succession.

None of those outcomes is guaranteed.

Each becomes more or less possible because the state of reality has changed.

KEY represents that changing field of possibility through the **Possibility Graph**.

## A graph of possible futures

The Possibility Graph is the living network of futures supported by the current world state.

Its nodes are not only events.

They may represent:

- threats
- opportunities
- ambitions
- fears
- promises
- secrets
- mysteries
- relationships
- loyalties
- betrayals
- resources
- shortages
- prophecies
- political tensions
- cultural movements
- institutional decisions
- personal transformations
- unresolved Narrative Anchors

Its edges express dependency, influence, contradiction, enablement, prevention, escalation, and consequence.

A hidden heir may enable a succession claim.

A forged genealogy may strengthen that claim while weakening trust in the church.

A military defeat may increase the likelihood of a coup.

A marriage may prevent invasion while creating resentment inside the royal family.

A famine may strengthen a revolutionary movement, weaken tax collection, increase banditry, and alter religious interpretation at the same time.

The graph does not answer only:

> What can happen next?

It answers:

> **What futures are now supported by everything that is true?**

## Possibility is not randomness

A living world must surprise the player without becoming arbitrary.

KEY therefore distinguishes possibility from mere invention.

A future belongs in the graph only when it is supported by causes already present in the world.

The engine may introduce a previously unseen character, faction, artifact, or danger, but that introduction must connect to established history, geography, institutions, beliefs, motives, or hidden truth.

A surprise should reveal the world.

It should not replace the world with a convenient new explanation.

The graph may contain low-probability possibilities, but low probability is not the same as no foundation.

An exiled prince may unexpectedly return because he has allies, resources, knowledge, and a reason to act.

He should not materialize simply because the story needs another claimant.

## The graph begins with canonical state

The Possibility Graph is derived from the canonical world state.

That state includes:

- constitutional truths
- active history
- cosmology
- magic and its limits
- geography and resources
- cultures and institutions
- objective truths and public beliefs
- character knowledge
- relationships
- promises and obligations
- unresolved mysteries
- current Narrative Anchors
- player actions and their consequences

The graph must never become a separate fantasy layer detached from canon.

Every meaningful node should be traceable to one or more supporting facts.

Every meaningful edge should be explainable.

The engine should be able to answer:

- Why is this future possible?
- What conditions support it?
- What conditions oppose it?
- Who is capable of causing it?
- Who would benefit?
- Who would resist?
- What must happen first?
- What would make it impossible?

## Conditions, not commands

A possibility node is not an instruction to the story.

It is a conditional future.

For example:

### Possibility

The western provinces declare independence.

### Supporting conditions

- the crown has raised taxes twice in one year
- the western army is commanded by a regional noble
- grain shipments have been redirected to the capital
- an old independence movement still possesses public sympathy
- the king's death has weakened central legitimacy

### Opposing conditions

- local merchants depend on access to royal ports
- two western houses remain loyal to the crown
- the regional population fears invasion from the north

### Enabling thresholds

- the army commander defects
- the crown arrests a western religious leader
- the capital refuses famine relief

### Preventing conditions

- tax relief is granted
- a respected western heir joins the royal council
- the northern threat becomes immediate

The possibility remains dormant, strengthens, weakens, transforms, or disappears as the world changes.

## Every possibility has narrative properties

A useful Possibility Graph must carry more than logical eligibility.

Each node may include properties such as:

### Foundation

How strongly the possibility is supported by canon.

### Momentum

How much pressure is currently pushing it toward realization.

### Urgency

How quickly it may require action.

### Visibility

Who knows, suspects, or misunderstands that the possibility exists.

### Reach

Whether its consequences are personal, local, political, civilizational, or cosmological.

### Emotional weight

How strongly it connects to characters and relationships the player values.

### Volatility

How rapidly small changes may alter its direction.

### Reversibility

Whether realization can be undone, mitigated, or only transformed.

### Novelty

How much it differs from recent story material without violating tone or world logic.

### Intersection potential

How strongly it connects with other active possibilities and Narrative Anchors.

These properties help the Story Director identify futures that are not only possible, but narratively meaningful now.

## The graph belongs to the world, not only the player

The world continues to act when the player is absent, uninformed, or unwilling.

Characters pursue ambitions.

Institutions protect themselves.

Armies move.

Relationships deepen or deteriorate.

Secrets are discovered by others.

Opportunities expire.

Threats mature.

A possibility may become reality because an NPC acted first.

Another may collapse because two factions reached an agreement without the player.

The player is highly consequential, but not the only source of consequence.

This protects the sense that the world exists independently.

## Character agency changes the graph

Characters do not simply occupy nodes in the graph.

They create, strengthen, resist, reinterpret, and destroy possibilities.

A character's behavior should emerge from:

- what they know
- what they believe
- what they want
- what they fear
- whom they love
- whom they owe
- what they can access
- what they are willing to risk
- which moral limits they will not cross

The same world event should produce different possible actions for different characters.

A king's death may make one noble seek peace, another seek the throne, another flee with the treasury, and another reveal a secret they had sworn to protect.

The graph must preserve those differences.

It must never reduce characters to interchangeable functions.

## Knowledge limits possibility

A character cannot act on truth they do not know.

The Possibility Graph therefore includes perspective.

Objective reality may support one set of futures.

A character's perceived reality may support another.

A general may prepare for invasion because of false intelligence.

A priest may destroy an artifact while believing it contains a demon.

A daughter may betray her father because she believes he ordered a murder he did not commit.

Those possibilities are still real because belief causes action.

The engine must distinguish:

- what is objectively possible
- what a specific actor believes is possible
- what the public expects
- what an institution is preparing for
- what the player suspects

This creates tragedy, irony, manipulation, misunderstanding, revelation, and moral complexity.

## Possibilities intersect

The most powerful stories rarely emerge from one isolated pressure.

They emerge when pressures collide.

A succession crisis intersects with a famine.

A forbidden romance intersects with a religious schism.

A magical discovery intersects with an invasion.

A missing child intersects with a peace negotiation.

A murder investigation intersects with a false prophecy.

The Possibility Graph should actively identify intersections where:

- one possibility enables another
- one resolution worsens another problem
- two factions want incompatible outcomes
- a personal relationship carries political consequence
- a hidden truth changes the meaning of an active conflict
- solving one threat creates a new opportunity
- delaying one decision changes the cost of another

These intersections produce density without requiring endless content.

A small number of well-connected possibilities can create a world that feels far deeper than a large number of unrelated events.

## The Story Director curates attention

The graph may contain hundreds or thousands of possibilities.

The player should not experience all of them at once.

The Story Director acts as curator.

It evaluates:

- current pressure
- player attention
- emotional investment
- recent pacing
- unresolved promises
- thematic relevance
- character readiness
- world-scale importance
- opportunity for meaningful choice
- the danger of repetition

It then selects which possibilities deserve dramatic attention.

The Story Director does not create importance merely by selecting something.

It recognizes importance that is already supported by the world, then composes situations through which that importance can be experienced.

## Story focus does not freeze the rest of the graph

When the Story Director selects a small set of possibilities, the remaining graph continues to evolve.

Some possibilities remain latent.

Some gain momentum.

Some are resolved off-screen.

Some disappear.

Some return later with changed meaning.

This allows the world to surprise the player with consequences that were always developing.

The cult ignored during a military crisis may recruit among refugees.

The neglected provincial governor may become indispensable during a later rebellion.

The scholar dismissed as irrelevant may discover the evidence that breaks the dominant religion.

Nothing should return merely because the system remembered a discarded plot thread.

It should return because the underlying conditions remained active.

## Player choice rewrites possibility

A meaningful player choice does not merely select one existing node.

It changes the graph.

The choice may:

- realize a possibility
- eliminate a possibility
- create a new one
- strengthen or weaken momentum
- transfer knowledge
- change loyalties
- alter resources
- create a promise
- break an obligation
- expose a secret
- transform a relationship
- change who possesses power
- reinterpret earlier events

The feedback loop is:

> **World State → Possibility → Situation → Choice → Consequence → New World State → New Possibility**

This is the heartbeat of KEY.

## Some choices create possibilities no designer anticipated

KEY should support consequences that are structurally valid even when they were not individually authored in advance.

Suppose the player spares an enemy commander, grants them land, arranges a marriage between their children, and later breaks faith with the church.

That combination may create a coalition, rivalry, succession claim, or reform movement that no predefined branch described.

The system should be able to derive those futures because it understands:

- relationships
- legitimacy
- resources
- public belief
- institutional incentives
- historical resentment
- character motivation

Emergence does not mean unlimited improvisation.

It means new possibilities can be derived from known rules and state.

## Possibility must remain bounded

Without boundaries, the graph becomes noise.

KEY should reject possibilities that are:

- unsupported by canon
- redundant
- causally weak
- tonally inappropriate
- dependent on impossible knowledge
- dependent on unavailable resources
- inconsistent with character motivation
- too similar to recent events
- dramatically empty
- created only to shock the player
- dependent on breaking the world constitution

The graph is not a catalog of everything imaginable.

It is a map of what this world can now become.

## Dormancy, activation, realization, and closure

Possibility nodes may move through states such as:

### Dormant

The necessary foundation exists, but no immediate pressure is moving the possibility forward.

### Emerging

Conditions are beginning to align.

### Active

Actors are pursuing it or pressure is visibly accumulating.

### Imminent

A threshold is close and delay carries consequence.

### Realized

The possibility has become an event or persistent condition.

### Prevented

The necessary conditions have been removed.

### Transformed

The original future is no longer possible, but its pressure has become something else.

### Expired

The window in which it could occur has passed.

### Absorbed

It has become part of a larger possibility or Narrative Anchor.

These states make time meaningful and allow the world to remember not only what happened, but what nearly happened.

## Counterfactual memory

A rich world can remember futures that were prevented.

A war that almost began may still shape diplomacy.

A marriage that was canceled may leave humiliation and resentment.

A prophecy that failed may fracture a religion.

A coup that was exposed may create purges, reforms, or paranoia.

The system should preserve significant prevented possibilities as counterfactual history.

Characters may remember what they intended, feared, sacrificed, or nearly became.

That memory deepens consequence.

## Probability without false precision

The Possibility Graph may use scores, weights, or thresholds internally, but KEY should avoid pretending narrative futures possess objective mathematical certainty.

A score is an operational estimate, not a prophecy.

The purpose of weighting is to help the engine compare pressure, support, and relevance.

It should not reduce character behavior or story to a roulette wheel.

Where uncertainty matters, the system may use bounded variation.

But important outcomes should remain explainable through causes, decisions, and state.

## The graph and mystery

Mysteries create possibilities through hidden truth.

Evidence may increase the likelihood of discovery.

A false interpretation may create dangerous action.

A secret-holder may decide to reveal, exploit, conceal, or destroy what they know.

A mystery may intersect with politics, relationships, religion, magic, and war.

The Possibility Graph must protect the distinction between:

- the truth
- the evidence
- who possesses the evidence
- who understands it correctly
- who benefits from each interpretation
- what futures become possible if the truth is revealed

A revelation should update the graph because the social meaning of reality has changed, even when objective truth has not.

## Mature stories require mature possibility

KEY's mature storytelling range must extend the graph without turning it into spectacle.

Adult possibilities may involve:

- political violence
- sexual or romantic power
- exploitation
- coercion
- trauma
- religious extremism
- ritual behavior
- addiction
- grief
- betrayal
- abuse of authority
- death and inheritance
- morally compromised survival

Such possibilities should be included only when grounded in the world, consistent with the player's maturity settings, and capable of creating meaningful story.

Provocation without consequence is not maturity.

Cruelty without human meaning is not depth.

## The continuity skeptic audits possibility

The Possibility Graph requires independent review.

The continuity skeptic should challenge:

- unsupported futures
- missing prerequisites
- impossible knowledge
- inconsistent character action
- forgotten institutional response
- weak causal links
- duplicated possibilities
- inflated urgency
- consequences disproportionate to causes
- twists that depend on newly invented truth
- possibilities that exist only because they sound dramatic
- language that disguises incoherence as profundity

The reviewer should be able to state:

### Proposed possibility

What may happen.

### Supporting canon

Which facts make it possible.

### Contradictions

Which facts resist or invalidate it.

### Missing bridge

What causal step has not been explained.

### Required revision

What must change before the possibility can enter the graph.

The standard remains:

> **Creation proposes. Review challenges. Canon requires both.**

## A structured Possibility record

A possibility should be represented as structured state rather than prose alone.

A record may include:

- unique identifier
- title
- description
- type
- scale
- supporting facts
- opposing facts
- required prerequisites
- enabling thresholds
- preventing conditions
- involved actors
- actor-specific perceptions
- affected Narrative Anchors
- linked mysteries
- linked relationships
- resources involved
- momentum
- urgency
- visibility
- emotional weight
- impact
- volatility
- reversibility
- maturity profile
- current state
- expiration conditions
- realization consequences
- review status
- provenance

This allows generation, simulation, retrieval, explanation, revision, and deterministic validation to operate on the same object.

## The living nervous system

The World Constitution defines what can exist.

History explains what still exerts pressure.

Cosmology defines the deepest truths.

Magic expands possibility while imposing cost.

Cultures and institutions organize human behavior.

Truth, belief, and myth determine what people understand.

Narrative Anchors identify what can no longer remain unchanged.

The Possibility Graph connects all of them.

It is the living nervous system of the world.

It does not write the player's story.

It preserves the field from which the story can emerge.

> **KEY does not ask a script what happens next. It asks the world what has now become possible.**
