# Truth, Belief, and Myth

## A world is shaped by more than what is true

KEY begins from a simple distinction:

> **Truth describes what is. Belief describes what people think is. Myth describes what a people cannot afford to forget.**

A living world requires all three.

Objective truth gives the world coherence. Belief gives characters perspective. Myth gives cultures memory, identity, justification, warning, hope, and meaning.

These layers must never be collapsed into one another.

A kingdom may believe its rulers descend from the sun while the royal bloodline actually began through conquest and forgery.

A village may tell a story about a river spirit because an ancient flood destroyed every settlement except theirs.

A priest may sincerely defend a doctrine that began as political compromise.

A rebel may expose a historical lie while misunderstanding the deeper truth beneath it.

The world becomes believable when people act not only upon reality, but upon their understanding of reality.

## Truth exists before interpretation

Every major question in a KEY world should begin with a canonical layer of truth.

That truth may concern:

- what actually happened
- how magic truly works
- whether a god exists
- who committed a crime
- why an empire collapsed
- what an artifact was made to do
- who possesses a legitimate claim
- what a prophecy originally meant
- which part of a ritual produces an effect

The truth does not need to be known by any living character.

It may be fragmented, buried, deliberately erased, or permanently inaccessible.

But the engine must know what kind of uncertainty exists.

There is a profound difference between:

- a truth that can be discovered
- a truth whose evidence has been destroyed
- a truth remembered only by an immortal witness
- a question with several plausible interpretations
- a question whose final answer does not exist within the world

KEY must preserve that distinction.

Mystery is not permission for inconsistency.

Uncertainty must itself have structure.

## Knowledge is always situated

Characters do not access the canonical world directly.

They know only what their position allows them to know.

Their understanding is shaped by:

- personal experience
- education
- class
- culture
- religion
- profession
- language
- geography
- loyalty
- trauma
- prejudice
- institutional access
- deliberate deception

A court historian and a battlefield survivor may describe the same war differently without either one lying.

A farmer may understand the seasonal behavior of magic more accurately than a royal scholar while knowing nothing about its cosmological origin.

A prisoner may know the true character of a celebrated ruler better than the ruler's own ministers.

A child may repeat a superstition that preserves a fragment of truth no adult recognizes.

KEY should therefore track not only whether a character knows a fact, but:

- how they learned it
- how certain they are
- what they believe it means
- what emotional weight it carries
- whether they are willing to say it aloud
- what could change their mind

Knowledge is not a binary flag.

It is a relationship between a mind and the world.

## Belief is a force

Belief does not need to be objectively true to produce real consequences.

A population that believes the king has lost divine favor may revolt even if the gods do not exist.

Soldiers who believe a battlefield is cursed may break before the enemy arrives.

A family that believes an inherited illness is punishment may conceal it for generations.

A prophecy believed by enough people can shape the future even when the prophecy itself has no supernatural power.

Belief can create:

- legitimacy
- obedience
- courage
- shame
- prejudice
- solidarity
- sacrifice
- persecution
- rebellion
- economic value
- political stability
- collective panic

This means belief belongs in the causal model of the world.

The engine should treat widespread belief as a form of social reality.

## Myth is compressed history

Myths should not exist merely because fantasy worlds are expected to have legends.

A meaningful myth carries something forward.

It may preserve:

- a distorted historical event
- a cultural fear
- a moral lesson
- an explanation for suffering
- a claim to territory
- the memory of migration
- the justification for a social order
- a warning encoded in ritual
- an encounter with something genuinely supernatural
- the identity of a people under threat

A myth can be false in detail and true in function.

A story about a hero sealing a demon beneath a mountain may actually preserve the memory of engineers containing a magical catastrophe.

A creation myth describing humanity born from ash may preserve the history of survivors after a continent-wide fire.

A forbidden tale about a queen who married the sea may conceal an ancient political alliance with a nonhuman civilization.

The question is not simply, "Did this happen exactly as told?"

The deeper questions are:

- Why has this story survived?
- Who tells it?
- Who rejects it?
- What behavior does it encourage?
- What institution benefits from it?
- What truth might it preserve?
- What damage would follow if it disappeared?

## Myths evolve as they travel

No important story should remain identical across every culture and century.

As myths move, they are:

- translated
- shortened
- expanded
- moralized
- politicized
- localized
- merged with older stories
- stripped of inconvenient details
- adapted to new technologies or religions

Different versions should reveal the interests of the people preserving them.

One kingdom may remember an ancient conqueror as a unifier.

A neighboring culture may remember the same person as a butcher.

A priesthood may transform the conqueror into a saint.

A rebel movement may recover the suppressed account of the conquered.

The contradiction is not a flaw.

It is evidence that memory remains alive.

## Institutions manufacture and protect belief

Belief is not distributed equally.

Institutions influence which stories become official, which questions may be asked, and which memories disappear.

They do this through:

- schools
- temples
- archives
- law
- ritual
- censorship
- monuments
- calendars
- festivals
- titles
- maps
- public punishment
- control of language
- preservation of relics

An institution may defend a falsehood knowingly.

It may also defend a falsehood sincerely because its members inherited it as truth.

That distinction matters.

A conspiracy of deliberate liars creates one kind of story.

An institution protecting a mistaken belief creates another.

KEY should resist the lazy assumption that every false doctrine is maintained by villains who know better.

People often preserve false beliefs because those beliefs hold their world together.

## Truth has a cost

Discovery should not automatically be treated as moral victory.

A truth may liberate.

It may also destabilize a kingdom, destroy a family, collapse a religion, expose a vulnerable people, or remove the only story holding enemies at peace.

The player may discover that:

- the royal bloodline is illegitimate
- a beloved saint committed an atrocity
- an enemy culture was falsely blamed for a catastrophe
- the gods are real but indifferent
- the gods are false but the religion still protects millions
- a prophecy was manufactured to prevent a worse future
- a sacred ritual works for reasons no participant understands

The question should not end with, "Will you reveal the truth?"

It should continue:

- To whom?
- With what evidence?
- At what time?
- For what purpose?
- Who will be harmed?
- Who will exploit it?
- What new belief may replace the old one?

Truth enters history through choices.

## Lies must have structure

A believable lie requires more than a false sentence.

The engine should know:

- who created it
- why it was created
- who currently knows it is false
- who sincerely believes it
- what evidence supports it
- what evidence threatens it
- how it is taught
- what institutions depend upon it
- what emotional need it satisfies
- what happens when it begins to fail

The strongest lies often contain truth.

The most durable propaganda usually connects itself to real fear, injury, pride, or memory.

A fabricated story that has no psychological or political foundation should not survive for centuries merely because the plot requires it.

## Revelation should reinterpret the past

A strong revelation does more than provide new information.

It changes the meaning of what came before.

The trusted adviser was not simply absent during the assassination. They were protecting the assassin's child.

The sacred monster was not guarding treasure. It was preventing the artifact from awakening.

The annual mourning ritual was not created for the dead king. It was created to hide the date of a massacre.

When truth is revealed, earlier scenes should become newly legible.

The player should be able to look backward and recognize that the evidence was present, even if its significance was hidden.

This is how surprise remains fair.

## Some beliefs become true through action

Not every belief is merely an inaccurate description of the present.

Some beliefs are commitments that help create a future.

A kingdom may become united because enough people believe it can be.

A hero may become worthy because others trusted them before they were ready.

A prophecy may be fulfilled because people organize their lives around it.

A myth may inspire the recreation of a virtue that never existed as purely as the story claimed.

KEY should recognize the difference between:

- belief as error
- belief as interpretation
- belief as identity
- belief as social coordination
- belief as self-fulfilling force
- belief as moral aspiration

The world is not shaped only by facts.

It is also shaped by what people decide must become true.

## The continuity skeptic

Truth, belief, and myth are especially vulnerable to generated nonsense because evocative language can conceal weak logic.

Before these elements become canon, the continuity skeptic should ask:

- Is the objective truth clearly distinguished from interpretation?
- Does each belief have a plausible origin?
- Does the myth preserve or serve something meaningful?
- Could the belief realistically spread through the available institutions?
- Has the story survived in a believable form across time?
- Do competing versions reflect different interests and experiences?
- Is the lie supported by evidence, authority, repetition, or emotional need?
- Does the revelation follow from existing clues?
- Would exposing the truth create believable consequences?
- Does the language communicate a real idea, or only sound profound?

A generated myth should not be canonized because it is poetic.

A doctrine should not be accepted because it is dramatic.

A revelation should not survive review if it requires everyone in the past to behave irrationally.

> **Creation proposes. Review challenges. Canon requires both.**

## A structured truth record

For important claims, KEY should maintain a structured record containing:

- canonical truth
- truth classification
- discoverability
- surviving evidence
- destroyed evidence
- known witnesses
- public belief
- regional variations
- institutional doctrine
- private interpretations
- deliberate lies
- mythic versions
- beneficiaries
- threatened parties
- emotional significance
- revelation conditions
- likely consequences of exposure

This allows the engine to reason consistently about what is true, what is believed, and what each character can know.

## The end of Book II

Book II began by defining a world constitution.

It then established history, cosmology, magic, culture, kingdoms, and institutions.

Truth, belief, and myth complete that foundation by explaining how reality becomes understood, contested, remembered, and transformed by the people living inside it.

A KEY world is now more than a generated setting.

It possesses:

- laws
- history
- origins
- power
- societies
- institutions
- memory
- misunderstanding
- hidden truth
- contested meaning

The world is ready to produce story.

> **The world knows what is true. Its people know only what they have inherited, experienced, discovered, or chosen to believe. Story begins in the distance between them.**
