# Magic With Cost and Consequence

## Magic belongs to the world

Magic in KEY is not permission to ignore causality.

It is a force, practice, inheritance, relationship, discipline, wound, language, technology, covenant, or mystery that exists inside the world's constitution.

> **Magic should expand possibility without erasing consequence.**

A magical act may be wondrous. It may appear impossible to those who do not understand it. It may exceed ordinary physical limits.

But it must still belong to the world.

It must come from somewhere.

It must require something.

It must affect someone.

It must possess limits.

And when it changes what is possible, the rest of the world must respond.

## The source of magic shapes its meaning

A world must not begin with a list of spells.

It must begin with the nature of magic itself.

Magic may arise from:

- the structure of reality
- divine authority
- forgotten machinery
- living consciousness
- ancestral inheritance
- language or true names
- agreements with nonhuman powers
- sacrifice
- memory
- emotion
- geography
- celestial cycles
- the remains of a dead god
- damage left by creation
- something the world itself is trying to become

The source determines more than appearance.

It shapes who can use magic, how it is understood, which institutions control it, what its use implies, and why certain powers are possible while others are not.

If magic is borrowed from conscious entities, then every use may also be a negotiation.

If magic comes from memory, then mastery may require preservation, theft, or surrender of identity.

If magic is a scar left by creation, then every act may widen that scar.

The origin of magic should create story pressure.

## Access must have a reason

Not everyone needs equal access to magic.

But unequal access must be explained.

Access may depend on:

- bloodline
- training
- initiation
- spiritual compatibility
- patronage
- geographic proximity
- ritual knowledge
- artifacts
- social rank
- biological adaptation
- moral condition
- personal sacrifice
- chance exposure
- a relationship with a specific force or being

KEY must distinguish between rarity and arbitrariness.

An ability is not meaningful simply because few people possess it.

Its rarity should follow from the world's conditions.

If only one person can perform a certain act, the world should know why, suspect why, or contain a hidden truth explaining why.

## Every power has requirements

Magic should never become a vague answer to the question, "How did this happen?"

Each important magical capability should define its requirements.

Requirements may include:

- materials
- time
- knowledge
- preparation
- concentration
- emotional state
- physical endurance
- location
- alignment with a cycle or event
- consent
- authority
- proximity
- witnesses
- sacrifice
- irreversible commitment

These requirements create tension because power cannot always be used immediately, safely, secretly, or repeatedly.

A ritual requiring three nights beneath an open sky creates different stories than a gesture performed in an instant.

A healing art requiring the patient's true consent creates different moral choices than one that can be imposed.

Requirements turn powers into situations.

## Cost gives power weight

Cost is not merely a balancing device.

It is part of what magic means.

A cost may be:

- physical
- psychological
- emotional
- spiritual
- social
- economic
- political
- ecological
- relational
- temporal
- hereditary
- metaphysical

The cost need not be paid immediately.

Some magic may accumulate debt.

Some may transfer harm elsewhere.

Some may alter the user so gradually that the consequence is not understood until years later.

Some costs may fall upon people who never consented.

The strongest costs create moral pressure, not only mechanical limitation.

A ruler may possess the power to save a city while knowing that the act will poison a distant valley.

A healer may restore a dying child while surrendering one of their own memories.

A prophet may see the future only by becoming less able to live in the present.

The question should not only be, "Can this be done?"

It should also be, "What does doing it make true?"

## Limits preserve meaning

Magic without meaningful limits erases tension.

If any problem can be solved by an undefined power, then preparation, sacrifice, courage, and choice lose their force.

A magical system should define:

- what magic can do
- what it cannot do
- what is believed to be impossible
- what is possible only under exceptional conditions
- what may be attempted but not controlled
- what changes when a boundary is crossed

Some limits may be absolute.

Some may be constitutional truths of the world.

Some may be incomplete human understanding.

Some may be laws enforced by institutions or gods rather than by reality itself.

KEY must preserve the difference.

A limit that is secretly breakable can create mystery.

A limit that changes whenever the story needs it creates distrust.

## Exceptions must become events

Rare exceptions can be powerful.

They should never be casual.

If the dead cannot return, and someone returns, that event should produce fear, theology, investigation, political struggle, and consequences for the person who came back.

If no one can cross a certain boundary, the first crossing should alter the world's understanding of itself.

If an artifact performs what ordinary magic cannot, its existence should create questions about origin, ownership, danger, and precedent.

> **An exception is not a loophole. It is a historical event.**

The more fundamental the rule that is broken, the greater the pressure created by the exception.

## Capability changes civilization

Magic cannot be common in practice and absent from the structure of society.

Every major capability creates secondary consequences.

If healing magic is reliable:

- medicine changes
- childbirth changes
- warfare changes
- population changes
- religious authority changes
- access becomes political

If teleportation is common:

- borders change
- trade changes
- prisons change
- espionage changes
- geography changes in meaning
- distance may remain emotionally important while becoming economically weaker

If truth can be compelled:

- courts change
- diplomacy changes
- privacy changes
- resistance methods emerge
- people debate whether compelled truth is morally legitimate

If the future can be seen:

- rulers build institutions around prediction
- markets react
- prophets gain and lose authority
- enemies create noise and deception
- societies argue about fate and responsibility

The world must respond proportionally to what magic makes possible.

## Magic creates institutions

Power attracts organization.

Societies may create:

- schools
- guilds
- temples
- licensing systems
- inquisitions
- military orders
- research houses
- black markets
- hereditary offices
- prisons
- sanctuaries
- ritual professions
- insurance systems
- anti-magical technologies
- laws governing magical harm

Institutions do more than regulate magic.

They define who is considered legitimate.

A village healer and a royal physician may use similar powers while one is honored and the other criminalized.

A church may call one practice sacred and another heretical because of politics rather than metaphysics.

A state may outlaw unlicensed magic while secretly employing the strongest practitioners.

The struggle over legitimacy is part of the magic system.

## Magic creates class and dependency

Control over magic creates social structure.

Questions of access should affect:

- wealth
- labor
- military power
- education
- mobility
- inheritance
- marriage
- citizenship
- religion
- prejudice
- resistance

If magic is hereditary, bloodlines become political resources.

If magic requires expensive materials, wealth determines access.

If magic depends on institutional training, schools become gatekeepers.

If power appears unpredictably, governments may fear children before they understand themselves.

A magical world should contain beneficiaries, victims, intermediaries, and people who reject the system entirely.

## Mastery should change the practitioner

Growth in magical power should not be only a larger number or stronger effect.

Mastery should alter the practitioner's relationship with the world.

It may change:

- perception
- identity
- social standing
- moral responsibility
- vulnerability
- relationships
- physical form
- emotional range
- memory
- lifespan
- obligations
- what can no longer be ignored

A person who can hear the dead may become powerful, but may also lose the ability to experience silence.

A mage who perceives every possible outcome may become less capable of commitment.

A healer who feels another person's pain may become compassionate, detached, addicted to being needed, or unable to continue.

The most interesting progression increases responsibility alongside capability.

## Artifacts are compressed history

An artifact should not be merely a powerful object with a name.

It should contain history.

An important artifact should have:

- an origin
- a maker or cause
- an intended purpose
- a cost of creation
- known and hidden capabilities
- limitations
- prior owners
- a reputation
- people who seek it
- people who fear it
- consequences if it returns to use

Artifacts may preserve capabilities that civilizations no longer understand.

They may embody broken laws, dead gods, extinct sciences, inherited guilt, or unfinished promises.

Possessing an artifact should create pressure even before it is used.

## Magic may be understood unevenly

The existence of magic does not require universal agreement about how it works.

Different cultures may explain the same phenomenon through:

- theology
- philosophy
- natural law
- ancestral tradition
- mathematics
- spirits
- psychology
- metaphor
- political doctrine

Several explanations may contain partial truth.

Some may be false but useful.

Some may be deliberate propaganda.

Some practitioners may achieve reliable results through an explanation that is metaphysically incorrect.

KEY should distinguish between:

- objective magical truth
- tested operational knowledge
- institutional doctrine
- cultural interpretation
- superstition
- deception
- unresolved mystery

This allows magic to remain wondrous without becoming incoherent.

## Magic should create moral choices

The deepest magical questions are rarely about strength.

They are about permission.

Who may use this power?

Who bears its cost?

Can consent be given freely when the alternative is death?

Does the ability to alter memory ever justify its use?

Is a summoned being a tool, a partner, or a prisoner?

May a ruler preserve peace by magically suppressing rage?

Is it moral to resurrect someone who did not ask to return?

Can a dangerous knowledge be destroyed without becoming an act of tyranny?

Magic becomes story when power collides with values.

## The continuity skeptic must challenge magic

Magic is especially vulnerable to explanations that sound compelling while failing under scrutiny.

The independent continuity skeptic should test every major capability before it becomes canon.

The review should ask:

### Constitutional consistency

Does this power contradict the established laws of reality, death, identity, time, fate, or divine authority?

If it does, has the contradiction been established as a meaningful exception with sufficient consequences?

### Causal sufficiency

Does the explanation actually account for the effect?

Does the system merely state that a symbol, emotion, bloodline, or ritual creates power without establishing why that relationship exists?

Not every mechanism must be fully explained to the player, but the canonical structure must remain internally defensible.

### Social consequence

Would a capability this powerful have altered government, trade, war, medicine, religion, labor, crime, or family life?

If not, why not?

### Scarcity

If the power is rare, what maintains that rarity?

Can the process be copied, taught, stolen, industrialized, inherited, or forced?

### Character knowledge

Does a practitioner understand only what they have learned, or are they speaking with the knowledge of the generating system?

### Cost consistency

Is the stated cost actually enforced?

Does the cost matter, or is it forgotten whenever inconvenient?

### Scope control

Could the power solve problems far beyond its intended use?

What prevents obvious exploits?

### Language clarity

Does the description communicate an intelligible idea, or does it rely on poetic language that only appears meaningful?

A phrase may remain mysterious.

It may not remain logically empty.

### Narrative necessity

Does the power create pressure, choice, discovery, or consequence?

Or does it exist only because it sounds impressive?

The skeptic should not remove wonder.

The skeptic protects wonder from becoming nonsense.

## The magical record

For engine purposes, each major magical system or capability should possess a structured record containing:

- source
- governing principles
- access conditions
- requirements
- capabilities
- limitations
- absolute prohibitions
- known exceptions
- costs
- delayed consequences
- social effects
- institutional control
- cultural interpretations
- historical uses
- related mysteries
- character knowledge boundaries
- continuity risks
- audit status

This record allows scenes, characters, institutions, and consequences to reference the same canonical truth.

## Power must leave evidence

Magic should leave traces.

Those traces may be:

- physical
- emotional
- ecological
- spiritual
- political
- historical
- relational
- perceptual

A battlefield changed by magic should not look untouched the next morning unless concealment is itself part of the event.

A person who paid a severe cost should not behave as though nothing happened.

A society that survived magical catastrophe should carry memory in law, architecture, ritual, prejudice, and fear.

Power without evidence becomes spectacle without history.

## The standard

KEY does not ask whether magic is realistic.

It asks whether magic is truthful to the world that contains it.

A magical system succeeds when:

- its possibilities create wonder
- its limits preserve tension
- its costs create weight
- its consequences reshape the world
- its mysteries invite discovery
- its institutions reveal power
- its use creates moral pressure
- its exceptions become history
- its rules survive independent scrutiny

> **Every magical capability must create a social, political, economic, moral, personal, historical, or metaphysical consequence somewhere in the world.**

Magic is not an escape from the world's logic.

It is one of the most powerful ways that logic becomes visible.
