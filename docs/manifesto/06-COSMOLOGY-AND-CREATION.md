# Cosmology and Creation

## The deepest truths of a world shape everything above them

Every world rests on answers to questions its people may never fully understand.

What existed before the world?

Was creation intentional, accidental, violent, cyclical, or emergent?

Are the gods real?

What happens after death?

Does consciousness belong to the body, the soul, the world, or something older than all three?

Is fate a law, a belief, a manipulation, or an illusion?

In KEY, cosmology is not decorative mythology placed above the story. It is the deepest causal layer of the world.

> **Cosmology should shape how a world understands life, death, power, meaning, and destiny.**

A creation story matters because it influences religion, morality, magic, authority, burial, inheritance, prophecy, war, sacrifice, and hope.

If cosmology can be removed without changing the world beneath it, it was never truly part of the world.

## Creation must leave fingerprints

The origin of a world should remain visible in the world that followed.

If creation occurred through divine sacrifice, sacrifice may become sacred, political, magical, or culturally necessary.

If the world formed from the remains of a dead celestial being, mountains, oceans, magic, and living species may carry traces of that being.

If reality was built as a prison, its laws may reveal containment, surveillance, boundaries, or recurring attempts at escape.

If the world emerged from conflict between opposing forces, that conflict may persist within nature, institutions, magic, and individual consciousness.

If creation was accidental, cultures may struggle with the possibility that meaning was not granted from above and must instead be created through action.

Creation must produce consequences.

The oldest truth should still exert pressure on the newest moment.

## Objective cosmology and believed cosmology are not the same

KEY must distinguish between what is cosmologically true and what civilizations believe.

A world may contain:

- objective cosmological truth
- divine or extradimensional testimony
- cultural creation stories
- religious doctrine
- philosophical interpretation
- political appropriation
- suppressed knowledge
- false revelation
- competing translations
- deliberate fabrication
- permanently unknowable questions

A religion may be wrong about creation and still be morally profound.

A god may be real and misunderstood.

A myth may be factually inaccurate while preserving the emotional truth of an ancient catastrophe.

A ruler may use a genuine cosmological event to justify a false claim of divine authority.

A skeptical culture may reject gods that objectively exist because those beings do not match the culture's definition of divinity.

This separation creates mystery without reducing belief to foolishness.

People should believe for understandable reasons.

## Gods require definition

The word *god* should never function as an undefined shortcut.

For each world, KEY should determine what beings called gods actually are.

They may be:

- creators
- firstborn intelligences
- ascended mortals
- embodiments of natural or conceptual forces
- fragments of a larger consciousness
- powerful beings mistaken for creators
- entities sustained by belief
- custodians of laws they did not create
- survivors of an earlier reality
- inventions given power through collective ritual
- absent, imprisoned, dead, divided, or sleeping
- entirely unreal despite widespread belief

The world constitution should define what gods can and cannot do.

Can they act directly?

Can they lie?

Can they die?

Do they require worship?

Can they create souls, or only shape bodies?

Are they bound by time?

Can they perceive every place, or only places connected to their domain?

What prevents them from resolving every conflict personally?

Power without limits destroys dramatic credibility.

Divinity becomes meaningful when it possesses nature, constraint, desire, and consequence.

## Presence and silence both require reasons

A world with active gods must account for their intervention.

A world with silent gods must account for their absence.

Silence may mean:

- the gods are dead
- the gods are bound by covenant
- direct action damages reality
- divine perception is limited
- the gods communicate only through unreliable vessels
- the gods disagree
- the gods no longer care
- the gods are waiting for a condition to be met
- the beings worshipped as gods never possessed the power attributed to them
- silence itself is part of a test, prison, cycle, or deception

The answer need not be known to the player.

It must be coherent within the world.

## Death must belong to the cosmology

Death cannot be treated as a universal assumption when the world has established souls, resurrection, reincarnation, ancestral memory, undeath, or divine judgment.

KEY should define:

- whether a soul exists
- when or how it forms
- whether all beings possess one
- whether identity survives death
- where the dead go
- whether memory survives with identity
- whether the dead can return
- what resurrection restores
- what resurrection cannot restore
- whether reincarnation preserves anything
- whether the dead can be contacted
- what prevents death from becoming trivial

If resurrection is possible, grief, murder, inheritance, warfare, religion, and political succession must all respond to that fact.

If the dead can advise the living, ancestry becomes an active institution rather than a symbolic tradition.

If souls can be damaged, divided, traded, imprisoned, or consumed, those possibilities must shape law and morality.

A cosmology of death must create a culture of death.

## Consciousness and identity require boundaries

KEY worlds may include possession, memory transfer, reincarnation, artificial minds, divine fragments, shared dreams, or bodies inhabited by more than one identity.

The world must therefore establish what makes a person the same person over time.

Is identity located in:

- memory
- continuity of consciousness
- the soul
- the body
- relationships
- moral pattern
- divine recognition
- legal recognition
- something else entirely

These questions are not only philosophical.

They determine whether a returned character is truly the person who died, whether a copied mind has rights, whether possession is murder, whether memory alteration changes guilt, and whether an immortal being remains itself after centuries of transformation.

Cosmology becomes story when metaphysical questions create human consequences.

## Fate must not erase agency

A world may contain prophecy, destiny, cycles, omens, divine plans, or fixed points.

But fate must not reduce the player to an actor reciting a completed script.

KEY should define what fate actually means.

It may be:

- a probable future perceived imperfectly
- a law that fixes certain events but not their meaning
- a self-fulfilling belief
- a divine attempt at control
- the memory of a previous cycle
- a pattern created by repeated human choices
- a branching structure with convergent pressure points
- misinformation presented as certainty
- a real force that can be resisted at terrible cost

Prophecy should create pressure, not remove possibility.

A prophecy may be fulfilled differently than expected.

It may be prevented while creating a greater consequence.

It may describe an event accurately but misunderstand its cause.

It may reveal what becomes likely if no one changes.

The player should never be protected from consequence by destiny, nor stripped of agency by it without the loss of agency becoming the story itself.

## Cosmology should create institutions

Beliefs about creation and existence should shape structures in the living world.

Cosmology may influence:

- kingship and legitimacy
- priesthood and law
- calendars and festivals
- burial and inheritance
- marriage and lineage
- treatment of outsiders
- education
- magical authority
- architecture
- warfare
- medicine
- punishment
- concepts of personhood
- attitudes toward nature
- obligations to ancestors
- expectations about the end of the world

A culture that believes each ruler inherits a fragment of the first sun will build different institutions from one that believes rulership is a temporary burden imposed by the dead.

A society that believes mountains are sleeping gods will mine differently, travel differently, and interpret earthquakes differently.

A civilization that expects the world to end every thousand years will organize power, memory, and sacrifice around that expectation.

Cosmology must enter daily life.

## Creation should generate conflict without dictating villains

The deepest truths of a world can produce incompatible values.

One faith may believe the world is sacred and must be preserved.

Another may believe reality is a broken prison that must be destroyed.

One culture may view death as reunion.

Another may view the afterlife as captivity.

One order may seek to restore the absent gods.

Another may believe their return would end mortal freedom.

These conflicts should not automatically divide truth from falsehood or good from evil.

Each position should emerge from history, evidence, experience, and interest.

Cosmological conflict is strongest when the truth does not make the moral choice easy.

## Some truths may be dangerous to know

Knowledge of creation may alter reality.

A name may grant access.

A remembered event may awaken something.

Understanding the nature of the soul may make souls manipulable.

Proving that gods depend on belief may destabilize both religion and the gods themselves.

Discovering that the world is artificial may create freedom, despair, revolution, or collapse.

KEY should define not only whether cosmological truth can be discovered, but what discovery does.

A revelation may:

- change religious authority
- invalidate a dynasty
- enable forbidden magic
- weaken or empower a god
- alter the behavior of death
- fracture a culture
- create a new institution
- transform the player's identity
- expose the world to an external force
- reveal that a supposed ending is actually a beginning

The greatest revelations should not merely answer questions.

They should change the conditions under which the world continues.

## Not everything should be knowable

A coherent world does not require total explanation.

KEY should preserve a distinction between:

- truth that exists and can be discovered
- truth that exists but may never be discovered
- truth that can only be inferred
- truth that changes depending on perspective or state
- questions for which the world itself contains no final answer

Permanent uncertainty can be meaningful when it is deliberate rather than evasive.

The player may learn what the gods remember without knowing whether the gods themselves were created.

They may prove that souls continue after death without proving why consciousness exists.

They may discover the mechanism of prophecy without resolving whether meaning is inherent in the universe.

Mystery should survive explanation at the edges of existence.

## Cosmological mysteries require layered evidence

A major cosmological mystery should not be solved through a single convenient document.

Evidence may appear through:

- incompatible sacred texts
- ruins older than accepted creation
- recurring symbols across isolated cultures
- divine testimony that contradicts physical evidence
- magical phenomena
- inherited memories
- astronomical patterns
- forbidden rituals
- impossible species
- places where reality behaves differently
- historical events whose cause was misunderstood
- entities that remember earlier worlds

Different evidence may support different conclusions.

The player may discover the truth, part of the truth, or a persuasive false interpretation.

The world must know the difference even when the player does not.

## Cosmology must pass independent scrutiny

Cosmology is especially vulnerable to language that sounds profound while saying nothing.

An independent continuity skeptic must challenge proposed cosmological canon before acceptance.

The review should ask:

- Does the creation cause actually explain the resulting world?
- Do divine limits remain consistent?
- Does the treatment of death match resurrection, inheritance, and grief?
- Do institutions reflect the beliefs attributed to them?
- Are metaphysical terms defined, or merely poetic?
- Does a prophecy preserve meaningful agency?
- Does the timeline allow the claimed events and religions to develop?
- Does a revelation follow from evidence already present?
- Is uncertainty intentional, or is it hiding an undeveloped idea?
- Would a skeptical player understand why people believe what they believe?
- Is an exception meaningful, or was it invented to solve a scene?

The reviewer should flag unsupported statements such as:

> The stars remember humanity because eternity was born from forgotten silence.

That sentence may be evocative, but unless *memory*, *eternity*, and *silence* possess established meaning in the world, it communicates no usable truth.

Poetry may deepen cosmology.

It cannot substitute for cosmology.

> **Creation proposes. Review challenges. Canon requires both.**

## The cosmological record

KEY should preserve a structured record of cosmology rather than only prose.

It should include:

- objective origin state
- creation event or process
- pre-creation conditions
- fundamental forces or laws
- nature and limits of divine beings
- origin and behavior of magic
- soul and death model
- consciousness and identity model
- fate and prophecy model
- known cycles or apocalypses
- objective unknowns
- discoverable truths
- dangerous knowledge
- cultural interpretations
- religious institutions
- political uses of cosmology
- contradictory evidence
- unresolved mysteries
- constitutional dependencies

This record allows the Story Director, World Architect, character agents, and continuity skeptic to reason from the same foundation.

## Cosmology should create stories at every scale

The same cosmology should be able to produce:

- a private crisis of faith
- a dispute over burial
- a succession conflict
- a forbidden romance across religious boundaries
- a magical discovery
- a political revolution
- a war between civilizations
- a confrontation with a god
- a threat to reality itself

The deepest truths of existence should remain connected to the smallest human decisions.

A grieving parent, a frightened priest, an ambitious ruler, and an immortal intelligence may all respond differently to the same truth.

That is where cosmology becomes alive.

## The principle

> **A world's creation is not its first piece of lore. It is the deepest cause from which its laws, beliefs, institutions, conflicts, and mysteries emerge.**

The player may never learn the whole truth.

The people of the world may misunderstand it.

The gods themselves may know only part of it.

But KEY must know what is true, what is believed, what is hidden, and what remains unknowable.

Only then can the world ask its greatest questions without becoming meaningless.
