# Knowledge Boundary Terms

These definitions extend the canonical vocabulary of KEY for Book IV, Chapter 18: Knowledge Boundaries.

---

## Knowledge Boundary

The limit of what a character, group, institution, or culture can reasonably know, infer, remember, believe, or access based on experience, relationships, education, perception, trusted sources, available evidence, and information pathways.

Knowledge Boundaries preserve mystery, prevent omniscient behavior, and ensure that choices arise from situated understanding rather than author knowledge or the Canonical World State.

---

## Situated Knowledge

The unique body of information, beliefs, memories, suspicions, interpretations, and misunderstandings possessed by an individual or group at a given moment.

Situated Knowledge may contain truth and error simultaneously. It governs what an actor believes has happened, what is happening, and which futures appear possible.

---

## Hidden Truth

An objectively true aspect of the Canonical World State that remains undiscovered, misunderstood, forgotten, concealed, denied, or inaccessible to one or more observers.

Hidden Truth exists before discovery. Revelation changes knowledge and interpretation, not the prior reality of the truth itself.

---

## Information Pathway

A causal mechanism through which information moves between observers, including perception, conversation, testimony, trade, education, writing, media, ritual, investigation, espionage, rumor, magic, memory, or deliberate deception.

Every transfer of knowledge requires a source, path, recipient, time, content, and interpretation. Information may be delayed, distorted, rejected, or transformed during transmission.

---

## False Certainty

Confidence in an inaccurate or incomplete understanding of reality that resists correction because it has become connected to identity, emotion, belonging, authority, belief, or power.

False Certainty is more than error. It is an error protected against revision.

---

## Epistemic Conflict

Conflict arising primarily from differing knowledge, evidence, memories, interpretations, assumptions, or trusted sources rather than from inherently incompatible goals or values.

Epistemic Conflict remains real even when the audience or system knows the objective truth, because the people within the world must still discover, interpret, reject, or act without it.

---

## Perceived Possibility

A future action or outcome an actor believes is available within their Situated Knowledge, emotional state, capabilities, and constraints.

Agency operates through Perceived Possibility. An option that objectively exists but cannot be known, imagined, or reached by the actor is not yet available as a meaningful choice.

---

## Epistemic State

The structured status of a proposition within an observer's understanding, such as known, believed, suspected, doubted, misunderstood, forgotten, rejected, concealed, or unknown.

Epistemic States prevent truth, belief, suspicion, memory, and deception from being treated as interchangeable.
