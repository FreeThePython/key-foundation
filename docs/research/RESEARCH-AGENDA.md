# The KEY Research Agenda

## Mission

KEY exists to discover, test, and operationalize the principles of Narrative Intelligence so living worlds can produce meaningful stories through truth, agency, memory, and consequence.

> **Understanding comes before generation.**

The research program does not assume that existing narrative theory is complete, that popularity is equivalent to quality, or that authored storytelling transfers cleanly into interactive worlds. It treats those questions as open problems.

## Grand Challenge

> **Can Narrative Intelligence produce stories that are emotionally meaningful, causally coherent, and structurally durable while preserving genuine agency inside a living world?**

## Research Pillars

### 1. The Science of Story

Study pressure, suspense, curiosity, wonder, revelation, sacrifice, catharsis, transformation, resolution, and meaning.

### 2. The Science of People

Study agency, situated knowledge, memory, belief, desire, fear, identity, relationship, loyalty, betrayal, growth, and legacy.

### 3. The Science of Worlds

Study truth, history, culture, institutions, ecology, economics, politics, religion, technology, scarcity, conflict, and possibility.

### 4. Narrative Systems Science

Study how autonomous agents, persistent worlds, causal state transitions, and adaptive direction can produce narrative without collapsing into either rigid scripting or meaningless simulation.

### 5. The Narrative Observatory

Build a comparative evidence base from films, television, books, games, plays, myths, oral traditions, tabletop campaigns, and interactive fiction.

### 6. Narrative Evaluation

Develop methods that distinguish structural craft, audience response, critical response, historical response, market context, and uncertainty.

### 7. Narrative Intelligence Architecture

Translate supported findings into requirements for the World Engine, Human Engine, Story Engine, Story Director, Continuity Skeptic, Consequence Engine, and Narrative Observatory.

### 8. Validation

Test the framework through bounded prototypes, benchmark worlds, comparative studies, adversarial cases, longitudinal play, and human evaluation.

## The Research Loop

```text
Observe stories and lived play
          ↓
Record structured evidence
          ↓
Form or revise hypotheses
          ↓
Compare across cases
          ↓
Translate findings into system requirements
          ↓
Build and test narrative systems
          ↓
Evaluate outcomes
          ↓
Revise theory and observe again
```

## Initial Research Program

### Phase I — Method

- establish the research methodology
- establish the case-study schema
- establish the hypothesis register
- define evidence and confidence standards
- define rules for disagreement and uncertainty

### Phase II — Pilot Corpus

Select a deliberately contrasting cohort rather than a collection of universally admired works. The pilot should include:

- acclaimed and commercially successful works
- critically praised but commercially weak works
- commercially successful but narratively criticized works
- works reevaluated over time
- broadly unsuccessful works
- at least one interactive narrative

### Phase III — Comparative Findings

- identify recurring patterns and exceptions
- test whether findings survive genre and medium changes
- distinguish correlations from plausible causal explanations
- document where KEY terminology fails to describe evidence cleanly

### Phase IV — System Translation

Convert findings into:

- schemas
- invariants
- design constraints
- evaluation criteria
- benchmark scenarios
- prototype requirements

### Phase V — Validation

Test whether KEY can preserve:

- agency without incoherence
- coherence without coercion
- consequence without determinism
- surprise without arbitrary invention
- emotional continuity without forced character behavior
- resolution without scripted inevitability

## Initial Open Problems

1. Which principles of authored storytelling survive genuine participant agency?
2. Can suspense emerge when no author controls the exact sequence of events?
3. Can revelations remain earned when discovery order is variable?
4. How much autonomy can supporting characters possess before narrative focus collapses?
5. Can thematic meaning emerge without being explicitly imposed?
6. Can a system distinguish transformation from mere state change?
7. How should contradictory audience responses be represented?
8. What evidence is sufficient to revise a constitutional principle?
9. How can narrative evaluation remain useful without becoming reductive?
10. Can a living world produce endings that feel complete while the world continues?

## Research Outputs

The program should maintain:

- research agendas
- methodology revisions
- structured case studies
- comparative studies
- hypothesis records
- rejected hypotheses
- open-problem records
- benchmark worlds
- evaluation instruments
- annual Narrative Intelligence reports

## Standard of Integrity

KEY research must be willing to discover that:

- a favored principle is context-dependent
- a proposed metric cannot be measured reliably
- an admired work succeeds by violating the framework
- a commercial failure contains strong narrative craft
- an audience favorite contains structural weaknesses
- a system improvement does not improve human experience

The Observatory is not designed to validate KEY. It is designed to make KEY harder to fool.

## Foundational Principle

> **KEY should build from evidence without reducing story to formula, and learn from tradition without becoming imprisoned by it.**
