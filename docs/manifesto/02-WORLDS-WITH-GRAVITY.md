# Worlds With Gravity

## A world is more than a setting

A setting is where a story happens.

A world is why the story can happen at all.

A setting may contain mountains, cities, kingdoms, ruins, creatures, and magic. A world contains the causes that made those things possible, the beliefs people formed around them, and the consequences that continue shaping life long after their origins have been forgotten.

KEY must never confuse visual richness with world depth.

A beautiful map can still feel empty.

A detailed history can still feel artificial.

A hundred cultures can still feel interchangeable.

A thousand generated facts can still feel weightless.

A world gains gravity when its parts pull on one another.

History shapes belief.

Belief shapes institutions.

Institutions shape law.

Law shapes behavior.

Behavior creates conflict.

Conflict creates memory.

Memory becomes the foundation of the next generation.

That chain is what makes a world feel alive.

## Gravity is created through consequence

A world with gravity does not merely contain facts. Its facts exert force.

If magic comes from the remains of a shattered moon, then the fragments should influence geography, religion, warfare, medicine, trade, status, fear, and language.

A kingdom built around the largest fragment may become wealthy, sacred, militarized, or cursed.

A culture living far from the fragments may regard magic as foreign contamination.

A religion may teach that using magic prevents the moon from becoming whole again.

A dynasty may claim divine legitimacy because its founder survived direct contact with a fragment.

A mining guild may control more political power than the crown.

Children born near an impact site may be revered in one kingdom and executed in another.

The original premise is not decoration.

It changes the world.

KEY should evaluate every major world fact by asking:

> **What pressure does this create elsewhere?**

If the answer is nothing, the fact may be atmospheric, but it is not foundational.

Atmosphere is valuable. Foundation determines what can happen.

## Worlds are built outward from causes

Traditional procedural generation often builds by addition.

Choose a biome.

Add a kingdom.

Add a religion.

Add a war.

Add an artifact.

Add a monster.

The result may be varied, but variation alone does not create coherence.

KEY should build through implication.

Begin with a small number of foundational causes and follow their effects outward.

A sea disappeared three centuries ago.

That may imply:

- abandoned ports standing hundreds of miles inland
- former naval powers struggling to survive
- salt deposits becoming a source of wealth
- displaced coastal peoples creating new cultures
- religious arguments over why the sea withdrew
- creatures left behind in the exposed depths
- borders drawn along coastlines that no longer exist
- ancient ships becoming sealed tombs
- a political movement demanding the sea be restored
- a hidden truth about what now occupies the empty basin

One strong cause can create geography, history, economics, faith, conflict, mystery, and future story.

KEY should prefer fewer causes with deeper consequences over more facts with weaker connections.

## The world must exist before the player enters it

A living world cannot appear to have been assembled only for the current adventure.

The player should enter in the middle of lives, arguments, ambitions, griefs, and unfinished histories.

A border dispute should have existed before the first court meeting.

A marriage alliance should carry expectations established years earlier.

A hero should have earned a reputation before meeting the ruler.

A village should remember the last war differently from the capital.

A religious order should contain internal disagreements that have nothing to do with the player.

The player becomes important because of what they do inside the world, not because the world waited silently for their arrival.

This means KEY must generate more than an opening situation.

It must generate inheritance.

The player inherits:

- debts they did not create
- alliances they did not choose
- enemies they may not understand
- traditions they may wish to change
- promises made by the dead
- wounds carried by the living
- myths that may conceal truth
- institutions that resist transformation

History is not background material.

History is pressure from the past.

## The past must remain active

A shallow world describes history.

A living world is still being shaped by it.

An old war should remain visible in borders, language, monuments, prejudices, missing bloodlines, ruined roads, military doctrine, and family memory.

A fallen empire should leave more than ruins.

Its laws may still influence courts.

Its language may remain the language of scholarship.

Its roads may determine modern trade.

Its crimes may be denied by its descendants.

Its lost technology may support institutions that no longer understand it.

Its collapse may have created the political order the player now inherits.

The past should not be complete.

It should survive as:

- memory
- myth
- grievance
- ritual
- inheritance
- architecture
- law
- debt
- fear
- unfinished consequence

When history remains active, exploration becomes more than discovering lore.

It becomes discovering why the present is the way it is.

## Geography must shape civilization

Geography should not function only as scenery or travel distance.

It should constrain and enable life.

Mountain ranges affect language, trade, defense, migration, and isolation.

Rivers create wealth, boundaries, agriculture, sacred sites, and invasion routes.

Harsh winters affect architecture, military campaigns, food storage, social customs, and political power.

A city built around a magical fault line should not resemble a city built on a peaceful coast.

A kingdom that controls the only safe mountain pass should possess diplomatic leverage even if it is small.

An island culture separated from the continent for centuries should carry different assumptions about ancestry, warfare, property, and the supernatural.

KEY must ask not only where people live, but how living there changed them.

The map should explain part of the culture.

The culture should explain part of the conflict.

The conflict should reshape the map.

## Cultures are systems of adaptation

A culture is not a list of clothing, food, names, architecture, and customs.

Those elements matter, but they are expressions of something deeper.

Culture is accumulated adaptation.

People develop customs because those customs once solved problems, expressed values, reinforced power, preserved identity, or explained uncertainty.

A society that survived centuries of monster attacks may value communal duty over personal freedom.

A culture shaped by unreliable prophecy may distrust certainty and prize public debate.

A people whose ancestors escaped magical slavery may treat all enchantment of the mind as an unforgivable crime.

A kingdom where the dead can occasionally speak may structure inheritance, justice, mourning, and political legitimacy differently from every other society.

KEY should build cultures around:

- environmental pressures
- historical experiences
- relationships with neighboring peoples
- access to power and resources
- beliefs about life, death, duty, identity, and authority
- institutions that preserve those beliefs
- contradictions between stated ideals and lived reality

No culture should be perfectly unified.

Every society contains classes, regions, generations, reformers, traditionalists, hypocrites, believers, outsiders, and people who simply want to live.

Internal difference creates authenticity.

## Institutions remember what individuals forget

Kings die.

Priests die.

Heroes die.

Institutions carry their intentions forward, often long after the original purpose has changed.

A knightly order founded to guard a sealed gate may become a wealthy political class after generations without invasion.

A council created to prevent tyranny may slowly become an oligarchy.

A religious ritual may continue even after no one remembers that it was originally a practical defense against magic.

A tax imposed during a forgotten emergency may become permanent.

Institutions create continuity, resistance, and unintended consequence.

They give the world structures larger than any individual character.

KEY should model institutions as entities with:

- founding purpose
- current purpose
- public justification
- actual incentives
- leadership
- resources
- internal factions
- traditions
- dependencies
- fears
- conditions under which they reform, fracture, or collapse

This allows the player to encounter systems that cannot be changed through a single conversation.

A ruler may command obedience.

They cannot command history to disappear.

## Belief can shape the world even when it is false

Objective truth matters, but people do not live according to objective truth alone.

They live according to what they believe.

A false prophecy can cause a real war.

A fabricated royal bloodline can sustain a legitimate dynasty for centuries.

A misunderstood plague can create persecution.

A god who does not exist can still inspire sacrifice, law, art, and empire.

A true god may be widely misunderstood.

The power of belief does not depend entirely on accuracy.

KEY must therefore distinguish between:

- what is true
- what is widely believed
- what institutions teach
- what individuals privately believe
- what people pretend to believe for safety or advantage
- what has been deliberately erased

This creates worlds where revelation has social consequences.

Proving a religion wrong does not instantly dissolve it.

Exposing a dynasty's lie does not automatically remove the ruler.

Discovering the truth may destabilize identities, laws, marriages, borders, and entire civilizations.

Truth matters.

Belief determines how truth enters the world.

## Contradiction is part of reality

A coherent world is not a perfectly orderly world.

Real societies contain contradictions.

A kingdom may worship mercy while celebrating conquest.

A republic may depend on inherited wealth.

A magical academy may claim neutrality while quietly deciding which kingdoms gain access to power.

A hero may value honor but remain loyal to a dishonorable ruler.

A culture may believe itself chosen while fearing that its god has abandoned it.

Contradiction does not weaken coherence when it grows from history, incentives, fear, identity, or human limitation.

It strengthens it.

KEY should avoid contradiction caused by carelessness.

It should embrace contradiction caused by life.

The difference is causality.

## Mystery requires hidden structure

A mysterious world is not one that withholds everything.

It is one where visible events suggest a deeper order.

A recurring symbol appears in ruins from civilizations that never met.

A royal family refuses to enter one wing of its own palace.

An ancient battlefield remains warm in winter.

Children in distant regions dream of the same door.

These details become compelling when KEY already knows what connects them.

Mystery should not be improvised backward after the player becomes interested.

The hidden truth should exist before the first clue appears.

Clues may be incomplete.

Witnesses may be wrong.

Evidence may be destroyed.

Interpretations may conflict.

But the underlying reality must remain stable.

A satisfying revelation does more than answer a question.

It reorganizes the player's understanding of the world.

It reveals that details once dismissed as separate were always connected.

That is narrative gravity pulling scattered events toward meaning.

## Magic must belong to the world

Magic should never feel like a collection of convenient abilities pasted onto an otherwise ordinary reality.

Its existence should alter civilization.

KEY must understand:

- where magic comes from
- who can access it
- what it costs
- what limits it
- how it is taught or discovered
- how institutions regulate or exploit it
- how ordinary people understand it
- how it changes labor, warfare, medicine, travel, faith, crime, and status
- what unintended consequences follow from its use

If healing magic is common, medicine, mortality, inheritance, warfare, and population should reflect that.

If teleportation exists, trade routes and borders should reflect that.

If the dead can return, law and religion should reflect that.

If magic requires memory, then power may erase identity.

If magic requires sacrifice, then every spell contains an ethical question.

The rules of magic should create stories, not merely solve problems.

Its limitations are often more important than its powers.

## Power creates ecosystems

No form of power exists alone.

Military strength creates fear, alliances, taxation, bureaucracy, and opposition.

Religious authority creates legitimacy, heresy, ritual, education, and resistance.

Magical knowledge creates schools, monopolies, black markets, regulation, and inequality.

Control of food, roads, water, trade, information, or prophecy can be as important as control of armies.

KEY should model power as a network rather than a single ranking.

A ruler may command soldiers but depend on merchants for funding.

A priest may possess no army but determine whether the ruler is considered legitimate.

A hero may hold no office but command the loyalty of the people.

A foreign scholar may know the one truth capable of destroying the kingdom's founding myth.

This creates political worlds where influence is distributed, contested, and constantly renegotiated.

## The world should continue beyond the player's sight

A world feels small when nothing happens outside the player's immediate attention.

KEY should maintain movement beyond the current scene.

Characters pursue goals.

Factions negotiate.

Borders change.

Rumors spread.

Children grow.

Old rulers weaken.

New beliefs gain followers.

Ignored threats develop.

A hero sent away should have experiences that do not exist merely as a reward screen.

A neighboring kingdom should make decisions based on its own pressures, not only in response to the player.

The player should sometimes discover that something important happened without them.

This does not diminish agency.

It gives agency context.

The player matters because they can change a world that does not depend entirely on their attention to exist.

## Not everything must become a story

A world with gravity contains more reality than the current narrative can use.

Not every ruin needs to become a quest.

Not every character needs a dramatic secret.

Not every institution needs to collapse.

Not every historical question needs an answer.

Excessive narrative density can make a world feel manufactured.

Some places should simply be old.

Some people should live ordinary lives.

Some mysteries should remain distant.

Some events should matter locally rather than cosmically.

The world must contain unused depth.

That unused depth creates the sense that reality extends beyond the current story.

KEY should generate abundance, but the Story Director should exercise restraint.

The purpose is not to expose everything.

The purpose is to make discovery feel possible.

## Detail should appear where consequence becomes personal

Players do not need to read hundreds of pages of generated history before they care about a world.

They need to encounter history through people and places.

A border war becomes real when a veteran refuses to forgive the neighboring kingdom.

A famine becomes real when a courtier hides food for their children.

A religious division becomes real when two heroes who love one another cannot agree where their child should be buried.

An ancient magical disaster becomes real when a village still rings bells every night to keep its memory away.

The engine may hold enormous depth.

The player should experience that depth through meaningful contact.

KEY should reveal the world at the point where lore becomes pressure, relationship, decision, or consequence.

## A generated world must still possess identity

Procedural variation can produce difference without identity.

Two worlds may contain different names, maps, and gods while producing essentially the same experience.

KEY must generate worlds with defining tensions.

A defining tension is a contradiction or pressure that reaches across multiple layers of reality.

Examples:

- Magic preserves civilization while slowly destroying memory.
- The gods protect humanity but require permanent political obedience.
- The empire ended war by erasing cultural identity.
- The dead can testify, but only through institutions controlled by the wealthy.
- Prophecy is accurate only when people attempt to prevent it.
- Dragons maintain the climate, yet their survival requires human sacrifice.

A strong defining tension influences cosmology, culture, politics, personal relationships, and the kinds of choices the player must make.

It gives the world a recognizable soul.

The player should be able to describe a campaign without relying only on proper nouns.

> "That was the world where magic consumed memory."

> "That was the kingdom built over a sleeping god."

> "That was the realm where the dead still voted."

Identity makes a world memorable.

## The world must be internally inspectable

KEY should not merely generate a convincing surface.

It must be able to explain the structure underneath.

For every major element, the system should be able to answer:

- What caused this?
- What does it affect?
- Who benefits?
- Who suffers?
- Who believes what about it?
- What evidence exists?
- What remains hidden?
- What could change it?
- What would happen if it disappeared?

This inspectability is essential for coherence, testing, editing, and future adaptation.

A writer should be able to explore the logic of the world.

A designer should be able to identify story pressure.

An engineer should be able to validate state.

The AI should be able to reason without inventing replacement facts.

The world should not be a paragraph.

It should be a structured reality capable of producing paragraphs, scenes, decisions, chronicles, and entirely different forms of expression.

## The measure of gravity

A world has gravity when a new fact does not remain isolated.

It changes how something else is understood.

A world has gravity when history is visible in the present.

When geography shapes culture.

When culture shapes institutions.

When institutions shape character choices.

When belief competes with truth.

When power creates dependence.

When discovery carries consequence.

When the player can change the future but cannot escape the reality they inherited.

A world has gravity when it feels larger than the story being told inside it.

When the player senses that every road came from somewhere, every title carries an old obligation, every ruin once had a purpose, and every person belongs to histories they did not choose.

KEY does not unlock collections of generated content.

It unlocks realities held together by cause, memory, pressure, and meaning.

That is what makes a world worth entering.

That is what makes it difficult to leave.
