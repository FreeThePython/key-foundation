# Book IV — The People

# Chapter 18: Knowledge Boundaries

## Opening

No one experiences the world as it truly is.

Every person experiences the world through the limits of their knowledge.

They remember imperfectly.

They misunderstand motives.

They inherit beliefs.

They trust unreliable sources.

They make assumptions.

They discover fragments.

They forget.

They lie.

The world possesses objective truth.

People possess perspective.

Story lives between the two.

---

## Reality Is Not Shared

Two people may witness the same event.

One sees justice.

One sees murder.

One sees liberation.

One sees invasion.

Neither is necessarily irrational.

They possess different histories, loyalties, fears, evidence, and trust.

Reality is singular.

Understanding is personal.

---

## Knowledge Is Situated

Every character knows only what they could reasonably know.

Knowledge emerges from where they have lived, what they have witnessed, who they trust, what they have studied, which institutions shaped them, what has been concealed, and what they chose to ignore.

Knowledge is earned through experience and transmitted through causal pathways.

It must never be granted merely because the story requires it.

A character does not reason from the Canonical World State.

They reason from a partial internal model of it.

---

## Ignorance Is Not Stupidity

People often lack information.

That is different from lacking intelligence.

A brilliant general may understand war but know nothing of court politics.

A farmer may understand weather better than scholars.

A child may notice truths adults overlook.

A priest may possess extraordinary theological knowledge while misunderstanding the needs of one grieving person.

Ignorance is contextual.

Characters should not become foolish merely to preserve mystery, create conflict, or delay a revelation.

Their errors should emerge from real limits, assumptions, incentives, emotions, and incomplete evidence.

---

## Belief Fills the Gaps

Where knowledge ends, belief begins.

Cultures create myths.

Families pass down stories.

Religions provide explanations.

Rumors spread.

Conspiracies emerge.

Hope invents possibilities.

Fear invents others.

People rarely leave uncertainty empty.

They interpret it.

Those interpretations may be wise, mistaken, manipulated, inherited, or sacred.

They still become part of the reality from which people act.

---

## False Certainty

The most dangerous person is not always the one who knows the least.

It may be the one who believes they know everything.

False certainty resists evidence.

It dismisses contradiction.

It punishes dissent.

It protects identity, authority, belonging, and power.

Knowledge Boundaries are often defended emotionally rather than rationally.

A person may reject truth not because it is weak, but because accepting it would destroy the story through which they understand themselves.

---

## Knowledge Changes Agency

Agency depends upon perceived possibility.

A prisoner unaware of an unlocked door cannot choose escape.

A queen unaware of betrayal cannot prevent it.

A physician unaware of a cure cannot offer it.

A child who believes every adult is dangerous may refuse the one person trying to help.

Characters choose from what they believe reality allows.

Not from objective reality.

Therefore, every meaningful choice must be evaluated against the actor's Knowledge Boundary.

A choice cannot be understood without asking what the actor knew, believed, remembered, feared, and considered possible at that moment.

---

## Hidden Truth

The Canonical World State contains truths no character may know.

Some remain hidden for centuries.

Some are known by one person and denied by a civilization.

Some are forgotten.

Some are actively concealed.

Some are visible but misunderstood.

Some may never be discovered.

Truth exists independently of observation.

Knowledge approaches it unevenly and incompletely.

The world does not become true when someone discovers it.

Discovery changes the observer, the Possibility Graph, and the futures that knowledge makes available.

---

## Information Travels

Knowledge is not static.

It spreads through observation, conversation, trade, education, ritual, books, songs, dreams, testimony, investigation, media, magic, rumor, espionage, confession, and propaganda.

Every pathway has limits.

Messages can be delayed.

Witnesses can be mistaken.

Translations can distort meaning.

Institutions can suppress evidence.

Stories can change with every retelling.

Information should never teleport through the world because the narrative would benefit from universal awareness.

Every transfer of knowledge requires a source, a path, a recipient, a time, and an interpretation.

---

## Misinformation

False knowledge can be as powerful as true knowledge.

A forged letter.

A false prophecy.

A mistaken identity.

An edited history.

A rumor begun by a frightened child.

A lie repeated by an institution until it becomes public memory.

People act upon what they believe, whether it is true or not.

Misinformation does not need to be perfectly convincing.

It needs to be plausible to the person receiving it within their existing Knowledge Boundary.

---

## Knowledge Creates Conflict

Many conflicts require no villain.

Only incomplete understanding.

One nation misreads another's defensive preparation as invasion.

A father misunderstands his daughter's silence.

A witness remembers incorrectly.

An ancient warning is translated poorly.

A friend conceals painful truth to protect someone and is later believed to have betrayed them.

Epistemic conflict emerges when people possess different evidence, memories, interpretations, or sources of trust.

Such conflict should not disappear merely because the audience knows the truth.

The people inside the world still must discover, reject, test, or survive it.

---

## Discovery Changes the World

Every discovery changes possibility.

Some discoveries answer questions.

Others create them.

Some free people.

Others burden them.

Knowledge expands agency.

It also expands responsibility.

A person cannot choose around a truth they do not know.

Once they know, inaction may become a choice.

The more someone understands, the more their decisions can carry moral, relational, institutional, and historical weight.

---

## Knowledge Is Never Complete

No civilization knows everything.

No archive is complete.

No map is perfect.

No historian is entirely unbiased.

No memory is flawless.

No institution preserves every truth it receives.

Some uncertainty can be reduced.

Some cannot.

The unknowable is part of reality.

Mystery should never disappear entirely, because a living world always extends beyond the understanding of any observer.

---

## Knowledge Boundaries Must Be Tracked

A Knowledge Boundary should not exist only as prose.

For significant people, groups, and institutions, the system should be able to distinguish:

- what they know
- what they believe
- what they suspect
- what they misunderstand
- what they have forgotten
- what they have been told
- which sources they trust
- which evidence they reject
- what remains inaccessible
- what they are concealing from others

These states are not interchangeable.

Suspicion is not knowledge.

Belief is not truth.

Memory is not a perfect record.

A lie told by a character is not automatically believed by its recipient.

Narrative Intelligence requires the system to preserve these distinctions.

---

## Story Director Responsibilities

The Story Director should not begin by asking:

> What does the player need to know?

It should ask:

> What does each person reasonably know right now?

> What can they infer from it?

> Through which pathways could knowledge change?

> What would this truth mean to them if discovered?

The Story Director may foreground opportunities for discovery.

It may bring people with conflicting perspectives into contact.

It may reveal the consequences of misunderstanding.

It may not grant knowledge without a causal path, erase uncertainty for convenience, or make characters omniscient servants of the plot.

---

## Continuity Skeptic Review

Before canonizing character or institutional knowledge, the Continuity Skeptic should ask:

- Could this person reasonably know this?
- How did they learn it?
- When did they learn it?
- Was the source reliable?
- What exactly was communicated?
- What misunderstandings remain?
- What biases shape interpretation?
- What has been forgotten or distorted?
- Could another person disagree honestly?
- Has hidden truth remained hidden where it should?
- Does new knowledge create new possibility and consequence?
- Has any character acted from information available only to the author, player, or Canonical World State?

Knowledge should feel earned.

Never convenient.

---

## Governing Principle

> A character may act only from the world they can perceive, remember, believe, and imagine.

Objective truth governs reality.

Knowledge Boundaries govern choice.

The distance between them creates mystery, error, conflict, discovery, responsibility, and story.

---

## Closing

Truth belongs to the world.

Knowledge belongs to people.

Belief belongs to cultures.

Memory belongs to history.

No one sees everything.

No one understands everything.

Yet every choice, every alliance, every betrayal, every refusal, and every act of courage is made from the small corner of reality each person calls truth.

And that is enough to change the world.
