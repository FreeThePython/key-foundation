# The KEY Lexicon

## Canonical Language for Narrative Intelligence

The KEY Lexicon defines the official meaning of foundational terms used throughout the manifesto, architecture, prototype, evaluation system, and future codebase.

These definitions are not marketing phrases. They are design constraints.

When a term appears elsewhere in the project, its meaning should remain consistent with this document unless the lexicon is deliberately revised.

---

## Canonical World State

The structured record of what is objectively true in a world at a given moment.

It includes entities, relationships, conditions, events, unresolved pressures, rules, knowledge boundaries, and causal history. It is distinct from what any character, culture, institution, narrator, or player believes.

The Canonical World State is the source from which valid possibilities and consequences are derived.

---

## World Truth

A fact that is objectively true within the world regardless of whether anyone knows, understands, accepts, or remembers it.

World Truth may concern history, cosmology, identity, causality, magic, relationships, institutions, or current conditions.

Truth may be hidden. It may not be arbitrary.

---

## World Constitution

The foundational system of truths, laws, limits, causes, and relationships that defines what is possible within a world.

The World Constitution includes cosmological rules, magical constraints, natural laws, historical foundations, and other governing principles that cannot be casually violated for narrative convenience.

It creates freedom by establishing coherence.

---

## Narrative Anchor

A structured pressure within the world that can no longer remain unchanged.

A Narrative Anchor identifies stakeholders, stakes, current conditions, clocks, thresholds, knowledge boundaries, and possible forms of transformation without prescribing one required outcome.

It creates direction without predetermination.

---

## Possibility Graph

The living network of futures supported by the current Canonical World State.

Its nodes may represent threats, opportunities, ambitions, secrets, fears, promises, relationships, discoveries, conflicts, and transformations. Its edges represent dependencies, incompatibilities, accelerants, blockers, and causal relationships.

The Possibility Graph does not contain every imaginable future. It contains futures the world has earned.

---

## Possibility

A future development that is causally supported by current world conditions.

A Possibility may become more or less likely as time passes, characters act, knowledge changes, resources move, and Narrative Anchors develop.

Possibility is not prediction and does not guarantee occurrence.

---

## Choice

An action, refusal, delay, silence, interpretation, delegation, sacrifice, or other exercise of agency that changes what is true, known, valued, possible, or likely within the world.

A selection is not meaningful merely because it appears in an interface. A meaningful Choice enters a living causal system and makes at least one future materially different.

Choice does not guarantee control over outcome.

---

## Consequence

A causally supported change that follows from one or more choices, conditions, pressures, omissions, or actions within the world.

A Consequence may be immediate, delayed, hidden, cascading, emergent, personal, relational, institutional, cultural, material, or narrative.

Consequence is not synonymous with reward or punishment. It is the world becoming different for reasons that can be traced.

---

## Consequence Record

The structured representation of a significant consequence and its causal relationships.

It may include initiating choices, contributing causes, responsible actors, affected entities, immediate and delayed effects, visibility, interpretations, reversibility, duration, resulting Narrative Anchors, and changes to the Possibility Graph.

A Consequence Record preserves the structure required for coherent future storytelling.

---

## Story Momentum

The changing dramatic and emotional energy created as pressures develop, intersect, transform, and resolve.

Story Momentum is not the quantity of action. It is the degree to which the story feels alive, directed, and increasingly meaningful.

It includes movement within characters, relationships, mysteries, institutions, Narrative Anchors, and the world itself.

---

## Narrative Resonance

The degree to which a moment connects with established choices, relationships, memories, fears, values, losses, promises, and meanings.

Momentum makes the story move. Resonance makes the story remain.

A large event may possess little resonance. A quiet moment may possess extraordinary resonance.

---

## Dramatic Readiness

The degree to which a possible moment is causally, emotionally, thematically, and contextually prepared to produce meaning.

Dramatic Readiness considers whether the world supports the event, whether characters can understand it, whether sufficient attachment or pressure has accumulated, and whether consequences can propagate afterward.

It does not permit the Story Director to delay reality merely to create perfect timing.

---

## Narrative Fatigue

The diminishing emotional and dramatic return caused by repeated patterns, scales, tones, devices, or forms of conflict.

Narrative Fatigue may arise from constant crisis, repeated betrayal, excessive combat, unresolved mysteries, spectacle inflation, or any dramatic technique used without meaningful variation or consequence.

---

## Mystery Graph

The structured representation of a hidden truth and the ways it may be approached, misunderstood, concealed, revealed, or transformed.

A Mystery Graph includes objective truth, public beliefs, interested parties, evidence, false leads, knowledge boundaries, revelation thresholds, and consequences of discovery.

Mystery begins with truth, not with a sequence of clues.

---

## Discovery

The acquisition of previously unknown information.

Discovery expands knowledge by answering or reframing a question. It becomes revelation only when the discovered truth reorganizes the meaning of prior events, relationships, choices, or beliefs.

---

## Revelation

The emergence of an existing truth that fundamentally changes interpretation without changing objective reality.

An earned Revelation was always true, was discoverable through the world, and changes meaning when understood. Revelation exposes canon; it does not retroactively manufacture it.

---

## Reinterpretation

The process by which existing events, choices, relationships, beliefs, and memories acquire new meaning after a Revelation.

Reinterpretation changes understanding while preserving the truth and causality of what already occurred.

---

## Reinterpretation Wave

The cascading effects of a Revelation as people, institutions, cultures, alliances, and systems revise their understanding and behavior.

The reach of a Reinterpretation Wave depends on who learns the truth, how credible it appears, which beliefs it threatens, and the Revelation's Narrative Gravity.

---

## Revelation Debt

The erosion of audience trust caused by unfair, unsupported, or retroactively invented twists.

Revelation Debt accumulates when a story contradicts established truth, withholds all reasonable paths to discovery, or uses surprise as a substitute for causality. As the debt grows, observation and curiosity lose value because the audience no longer believes evidence matters.

---

## Interpretive Stability

The principle that a Revelation may alter meaning but must preserve causality, canonical history, and the validity of prior choices.

Interpretive Stability allows the past to be understood differently without pretending that different events occurred.

---

## Narrative Gravity

A measure of how broadly and deeply a Revelation reshapes understanding across people, relationships, institutions, cultures, history, and future possibility.

Low-gravity revelations may remain personal or local. High-gravity revelations can transform civilizations or alter how reality itself is understood.

---

## Failure

A causally supported outcome in which an intended objective is not achieved, producing persistent changes to people, relationships, institutions, world state, or future possibility.

Failure is not punishment, discarded content, or an automatic reset. Meaningful Failure becomes part of canon and changes the future available to the world.

---

## Transformation

An enduring change in identity, relationships, institutions, beliefs, capabilities, or world conditions that emerges through experience.

Transformation may follow failure, loss, sacrifice, revelation, success, or sustained pressure. It need not be positive, but it must be causally and psychologically supported.

---

## Adaptive World

A Living World that reorganizes around changed conditions rather than waiting for a preferred state to be restored.

An Adaptive World responds to success and Failure through changes in behavior, relationships, policy, legitimacy, resources, belief, memory, and future possibility.

---

## Irreversible Event

An event or consequence that cannot be undone without violating canonical truth, causal integrity, or the meaning of prior choices.

An Irreversible Event becomes a permanent landmark in history. Future story emerges through adaptation to it rather than restoration of the world that existed before it.

---

## Productive Failure

A Failure that generates useful understanding, capability, warning, resilience, or new possibility despite not achieving its original objective.

Productive Failure does not erase loss. It preserves what the unsuccessful attempt taught the world and makes that knowledge available to future action.

---

## Cascading Failure

A Failure whose effects propagate through interconnected people, relationships, institutions, systems, or generations.

Each stage of a Cascading Failure must remain traceable through causal relationships in the Canonical World State rather than functioning as an arbitrary sequence of disasters.

---

## Earned Resolution

A stable and meaningful world state that emerges from accumulated truth, agency, choice, consequence, revelation, failure, irreversibility, and transformation rather than from a predetermined ending.

An Earned Resolution closes or transforms enough central Narrative Anchors to create genuine closure while preserving the continued life and future possibility of the world.

---

## Narrative Convergence

The process by which accumulated causality and irreversible change reshape the Possibility Graph until a smaller set of futures can honestly carry the meaning of what the world has become.

Narrative Convergence is not scripting or the elimination of agency. It is the emergence of coherence as unsupported futures disappear and earned Resolution Candidates gain Narrative Gravity.

---

## Resolution Candidate

A causally and dramatically supported future world state capable of resolving or transforming enough central pressures to create meaningful closure.

A Resolution Candidate is not a selected ending. It may strengthen, weaken, merge, transform, or disappear as Canonical World State continues to change.

---

## Narrative Equilibrium

A temporary state in which the dominant pressures of a story no longer demand immediate transformation in their previous form, allowing the meaning of accumulated change to settle.

Narrative Equilibrium does not require perfect peace or the absence of conflict. It creates closure while leaving the world alive and capable of future change.

---

## Historical Legacy

The enduring causal, cultural, institutional, relational, and emotional inheritance created by a story's resolution.

Historical Legacy records not only what the world became, but why it became that way, who shaped the outcome, who paid for it, how it is remembered, and which future pressures it creates.

---

## Open Future

The set of causally supported possibilities that remain after an Earned Resolution.

The Open Future preserves consequences still unfolding, relationships still changing, institutions still adapting, and new Narrative Anchors emerging from the world created by the completed story.

---

## Story Director

The interpretive system that curates attention, timing, contrast, escalation, quiet, intersection, and presentation from valid world state and possibilities.

The Story Director is a conductor, not the sole author of reality.

It may foreground, delay, intersect, or frame events. It may not invent unsupported outcomes, erase agency, or violate canon for dramatic convenience.

---

## World Architect

The system responsible for creating and maintaining the causal foundations of a world.

The World Architect establishes cosmology, history, geography, cultures, institutions, magic, conflicts, hidden truths, and other foundational structures before they are canonized.

Its proposals remain subject to independent review.

---

## Continuity Skeptic

An independent critical-review system that challenges proposed canon, narrative developments, and generated content before acceptance.

It audits causality, chronology, world rules, character behavior, knowledge boundaries, mystery fairness, institutional logic, emotional continuity, language, tone, and unsupported convenience.

Creation proposes. The Continuity Skeptic challenges. Canon requires both.

---

## Knowledge Boundary

The limit of what a character, group, institution, or culture can reasonably know, believe, infer, remember, or access.

Knowledge Boundaries preserve mystery, prevent omniscient characters, and ensure that decisions arise from situated understanding rather than author knowledge.

---

## Consequence Memory

The structured causal record of not only what happened, but why it happened and which prior choices, pressures, and conditions contributed to it.

Consequence Memory allows the world to remember choice through enduring effects, historical interpretation, institutional response, and future possibility.

---

## Realmprint

A portable, structured definition of a world or narrative reality that can be instantiated, shared, extended, audited, and expressed across multiple experiences or media.

A Realmprint contains more than lore. It preserves the world’s constitutional logic, truth, history, cultures, systems, possibilities, and canonical relationships.

---

## Narrative Intelligence

The capacity of a system to understand and maintain the relationship among world truth, character knowledge, motivation, pressure, choice, consequence, timing, memory, and meaning.

Narrative Intelligence is not merely the ability to generate fluent prose.

It is the ability to preserve coherence while allowing story to emerge.

---

## Living World

A world whose characters, institutions, pressures, and conditions continue to change according to their own causal logic rather than waiting for player activation.

The player may influence a Living World profoundly, but does not constitute its only source of action or meaning.

---

## Canonization

The process through which proposed world material becomes accepted as official truth.

Canonization should normally follow generation, audit, challenge, revision, re-audit, and validation against existing Canonical World State.

Generated content is not canon merely because it was produced.

---

## Narrative Maturity Profile

The structured set of player preferences and system constraints governing which mature themes may exist, appear, or be described, and at what degree of intensity.

It distinguishes subject matter from presentation detail and may separately govern violence, sexuality, horror, trauma, cruelty, language, psychological distress, and related themes.

Adult storytelling requires responsibility, context, and consequence rather than gratuitous explicitness.

---

## The Governing Standard

Every KEY term should help the system answer at least one of these questions:

- What is true?
- Who knows it?
- What is changing?
- What is possible?
- Why does it matter?
- What follows from choice?
- How does the world remember?

> **A shared language allows a philosophy to become an architecture, and an architecture to become a living engine.**
