# KEY Narrative Ontology

## Status

Version 0.1 — provisional foundation.

> KEY does not seek to reduce storytelling to formulas. It seeks to identify the persistent structures that allow creativity to produce coherent, meaningful, and surprising narratives. Every claim remains provisional and is expected to evolve through observation, comparison, and evidence.

## Purpose

The Narrative Ontology defines the canonical kinds of things KEY must represent in order to model worlds, people, institutions, causality, and narrative change.

It is not a glossary of literary terms. It is a formal vocabulary for research, architecture, simulation, and evaluation.

The ontology must allow KEY to answer five questions:

1. What exists in a narrative world?
2. What state does each thing carry?
3. What processes can change that state?
4. What rules constrain change?
5. What higher-order systems emerge from interaction?

## Design Principles

1. **State before prose.** Narrative meaning must remain representable independently of generated language.
2. **Situated agents.** People act from local knowledge, belief, memory, desire, fear, identity, and constraint.
3. **Persistent consequence.** Important events alter future state unless another event credibly transforms or resolves that change.
4. **Independent systems.** Institutions, relationships, cultures, and conflicts may continue without protagonist observation.
5. **Interpretive plurality.** Objective world truth, agent belief, public belief, and audience interpretation must remain distinguishable.
6. **Research traceability.** Ontological commitments remain revisable when Observatory evidence challenges them.

## Primitive Entity Types

### Agent

A situated decision-making being.

Minimum state:

- identity
- location
- physical condition
- knowledge
- beliefs
- memories
- desires
- fears
- values
- commitments
- resources
- relationships
- perceived possibilities

Agents need not be human, but they must possess a meaningful model of alternatives and some mechanism for selection among them.

### Institution

A persistent coordinated system whose behavior cannot be reduced to one current member.

Minimum state:

- purpose or dominant incentives
- membership
- roles and hierarchy
- rules
- resources
- territory or jurisdiction
- public reputation
- internal factions
- institutional memory
- active policies

### Relationship

A persistent, directional connection between agents, groups, or institutions.

Possible state:

- trust
- affection
- fear
- respect
- resentment
- dependency
- obligation
- power asymmetry
- shared history
- promises
- secrets
- unresolved injuries

Relationship state is not assumed to be symmetrical.

### Location

A place with physical, social, historical, and symbolic properties.

A location may contain:

- access constraints
- resources
- dangers
- inhabitants
- laws
- memories
- ownership claims
- routes
- cultural meaning

### Resource

Anything scarce, transferable, consumable, protectable, or strategically useful.

Examples include water, money, time, medicine, information, labor, legitimacy, attention, and military capacity.

### Artifact

A persistent object whose existence or possession can affect knowledge, identity, power, memory, or possibility.

### Event

A bounded occurrence that changes one or more states.

An event record should preserve:

- participants
- location
- time
- prior state
- action or process
- immediate outcome
- affected entities
- observers
- interpretations
- persistent consequences

### Knowledge

Information an entity treats as known, including provenance and confidence.

Knowledge must be separated from objective truth.

### Belief

A proposition an agent or institution accepts as likely or meaningful, whether true or false.

### Memory

A retained representation of an event, relationship, condition, or interpretation.

Memory may be incomplete, distorted, private, shared, suppressed, or institutionally recorded.

### Desire

A preferred future state.

Desire may be affirmative, avoidant, conflicted, inherited, hidden, or imposed.

### Fear

A negatively valued possible future that influences behavior before it occurs.

### Commitment

A self-binding or socially binding intention that changes the cost of future choices.

Promises, vows, duties, contracts, and declared loyalties are forms of commitment.

### Rule

A condition governing what actions or transitions are possible, permitted, probable, costly, or forbidden.

### Constraint

A current limitation on action or possibility.

Constraints may be physical, social, informational, psychological, institutional, temporal, or moral.

### Pressure

A force that increases the cost of maintaining the current state or narrows acceptable alternatives.

### Truth

A proposition represented as objectively valid within the canonical world state.

### Mystery

A meaningful gap between available interpretation and hidden or unresolved truth.

### Consequence

A persistent state change attributable to an event or decision.

### Possibility

A future transition currently available to an entity or system.

A possibility may be objectively available, perceived, hidden, impossible, or believed possible.

## State Model

Every persistent entity has:

- **canonical state** — what is true in the world
- **perceived state** — what an observer believes is true
- **historical state** — how it became what it is
- **latent state** — capacities or conditions not yet expressed
- **projected state** — expected or feared futures

Narrative Intelligence depends on preserving differences among these layers.

## Processes

Processes transform state through time.

Core process families include:

- perception
- learning
- forgetting
- interpretation
- communication
- deception
- movement
- exchange
- cooperation
- conflict
- injury
- healing
- creation
- destruction
- discovery
- concealment
- commitment
- betrayal
- institutional decision
- cultural transmission
- revelation
- failure
- sacrifice
- resolution

A process is narratively important when it changes future possibility, interpretation, relationship, identity, pressure, or consequence.

## Constraints and Transition Rules

The ontology assumes no universal literary laws. It does require explicit transition discipline.

1. Knowledge requires a source, inference, or justified prior state.
2. Events cannot affect unconnected entities without a propagation path.
3. Relationship changes require interaction, evidence, reinterpretation, or sustained pressure.
4. Institutional behavior follows incentives, rules, factions, resources, and history rather than authorial convenience alone.
5. Physical and temporal continuity must be preserved unless the world explicitly permits exceptions.
6. Resolved pressure may end while secondary consequences remain active.
7. A contradiction must be represented as error, deception, uncertainty, competing interpretation, or world change.

## Emergent Systems

Higher-order narrative systems emerge from interactions among primitive entities.

Examples:

- culture
- family
- economy
- politics
- religion
- war
- law
- class
- reputation
- tradition
- markets
- resistance movements
- mythology
- public memory

These systems should not be modeled as decorative labels. They must alter incentives, beliefs, relationships, resources, constraints, and possibilities.

## Ontological Distinctions

### Truth, knowledge, belief, and interpretation

These are separate objects. A world may contain one truth, several beliefs, incomplete knowledge, and many interpretations.

### Event and consequence

An event occurs. A consequence persists.

### Desire and goal

Desire is a preferred state. A goal is an operational target selected in service of one or more desires.

### Constraint and pressure

A constraint limits action. Pressure changes the cost of remaining unchanged.

### Possibility and perceived possibility

An option may exist without being visible to an agent, or appear possible while being objectively unavailable.

### Institution and group

A group becomes an institution when roles, rules, incentives, resources, or memory persist beyond immediate membership.

## Mapping to KEY Engines

- **World Engine:** canonical state, locations, resources, institutions, rules, active history, emergent systems
- **Human Engine:** agents, situated knowledge, belief, memory, desire, fear, identity, commitments, relationships
- **Story Engine:** pressure, possibility, selection, escalation, revelation, transformation, resolution
- **Memory Engine:** event records, consequence persistence, relationship history, institutional memory, provenance
- **Evaluation Engine:** coherence, causality, agency, consequence, interpretive continuity, uncertainty

## Open Questions

1. Which entity types are truly primitive and which are composites?
2. How should collective agents differ from institutions?
3. How should emotion be represented without reducing it to a single score?
4. How can symbolic meaning be modeled without confusing interpretation with canonical truth?
5. What level of state granularity produces useful simulation without making the system intractable?
6. How should unreliable memory and contested history propagate?
7. Which transition rules remain valid across cultures, genres, and media?

## Foundational Proposition

> Stories are not sequences of events. They are evolving systems of state in which events matter because they alter what entities know, want, fear, remember, owe, can do, and may become.
